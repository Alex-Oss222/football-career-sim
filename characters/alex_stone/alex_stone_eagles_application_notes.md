# ALEX STONE — EAGLES APPLICATION STRATEGY
## Candidate Brief: Philadelphia Eagles Offensive QC
## Sim Rule: Internal strategy doc. Not world-state — Stone's perspective only.

---

## WHY STONE WANTS PHILLY

**Not the easiest job. The best job.**

Cleveland and San Diego have lower barriers. But Philadelphia under Reid offers the strongest combination of realistic access, philosophical fit, development quality, and long-term upside.

Specific reasons, in order of weight:

1. **Reid hires teachers, not résumés.** Reid's stated criterion — "I'm not going to hire anybody who isn't a teacher" — is the most favorable hiring philosophy for Stone's profile on the entire 1999 board. No other HC has articulated a priority that aligns this directly with Stone's strengths.

2. **Reid's 1999 hiring pattern proves the philosophy is real.** He hired a D3 OC as an offensive assistant, a college TE coach with zero NFL experience for the TE room, a former player with two years of QC as his LB coach. Reid is not paying lip service to "character over pedigree" — he is living it.

3. **The WCO is the most transferable system credential in football.** Learning Walsh/Holmgren WCO gives Stone a schematic language that is valued across the league. No other system on this market carries the same long-term career value.

4. **Reid coached TEs.** He spent five years coaching TEs and OL in Green Bay (1992–1996). He understands the position at a coaching level. He will recognize Stone's TE expertise as legitimate, and he will value it in ways that HCs without TE backgrounds would not.

5. **Dowhower is a Coryell disciple.** The OC started under Don Coryell at San Diego State and later worked under Walsh at Stanford. He bridges Stone's playing system (Coryell) to Reid's coaching system (WCO). No other OC on this market has that bridge.

6. **Ground floor.** Reid is building from scratch. Stone enters at the same time as everyone else. No incumbent hierarchy. No established culture to penetrate. Everyone is new, everyone is learning, everyone is proving themselves.

---

## WHY EAGLES QC IS THE RIGHT ENTRY POINT

Not TE coach. Not "whatever you'll give me." Specifically: Offensive Quality Control.

- Stone meets the minimum requirements for QC. He does not meet them for position coach.
- QC is a learning role. Stone needs to learn coaching. The QC job is designed for that.
- QC puts Stone inside Reid's offensive structure — attending meetings, doing film work, absorbing the system — without the pressure of managing a position group before he knows how.
- The TE coach (Shurmur) has zero NFL experience. Stone and Shurmur can develop a symbiotic relationship — Shurmur teaches Stone how to run a room, Stone's TE knowledge helps Shurmur teach better technique. But this only works if Stone is not competing with Shurmur for the same job.
- If Stone enters as QC and performs, the TE coach path opens naturally. If Shurmur advances (to QB coach or OC), Stone is the obvious internal successor. But Stone earns that through performance, not through interviewing for a job he's not ready for.

## WHAT STONE SHOULD EMPHASIZE IN A COVER LETTER / INTERVIEW

### Lead with teaching, not playing.

Stone's playing career is the elephant in the room. Everyone knows who he is. He does not need to establish his football credentials — they are established. What he needs to establish is that he wants to coach, specifically to teach, and that he understands the difference between playing and coaching.

**Words Stone should use:**
- "teach" / "teaching" — this is Reid's keyword
- "learn" / "learning" — signals humility and coachability
- "start at the bottom" — but only if followed by genuine explanation of why, not as a throwaway line
- "system" — shows Stone understands he's entering someone else's framework
- "preparation" / "process" — Reid's operational values

**Framing Stone should use:**
- "I spent 12 years learning how to play the position. I want to spend the next phase learning how to teach it."
- "I've never run a meeting, graded film as a coach, or organized a practice. I know that. I want to learn those things from the ground up."
- "The West Coast Offense is the best system to learn in. I want to be in a building where the teaching is at the highest level."

### Demonstrate specific teaching vision.

Reid doesn't want to hear "I want to give back to the game." He wants to hear what Stone would actually teach and how. Stone should be prepared to:

- Describe a specific TE technique (route release, blocking leverage, stem mechanics) and how he would teach it to a young player
- Explain the difference between knowing a technique and teaching a technique — showing that Stone understands the gap
- Reference a moment from his playing career where a coach taught him something that changed his game — demonstrating that Stone values teaching from the receiving end, not just the giving end

### Show system curiosity.

Stone should demonstrate that he's already thinking about the WCO, even if he doesn't know it yet:

- "I played in the Coryell system. I know the WCO shares some concepts but is a different philosophy. I want to learn how the WCO thinks about the game, not just how it names plays."
- "I've studied some of what Coach Holmgren did in Green Bay. I'm interested in how you're adapting that for Philadelphia."

### Reference the QC role specifically.

Stone should make clear he is pursuing QC, not angling for a bigger role:

- "I want the QC job. Not because I think I'm too good for it, but because I think it's the right place to learn how coaching actually works."
- "I know the QC role is film breakdown, scout team, and whatever else needs doing. I'm ready for that."

## WHAT STONE SHOULD AVOID SAYING

- **Anything that references his playing career as a qualification for coaching.** His career qualifies him for nothing in coaching. It qualifies him for a Hall of Fame jacket. Coaching is a different skill set, and Stone must communicate that he understands this.
- **"I feel like I owe it to the game."** This is a retirement-hobby framing. Reid wants professionals, not hobbyists.
- **"I could probably help with the TEs."** This sounds like he's angling for the TE coach job or positioning himself above the QC level. Stone pursues QC and lets Reid decide where his TE knowledge adds value.
- **"I know a lot about the passing game."** Everyone on an NFL staff knows about the passing game. Stone's playing knowledge is not coaching knowledge. He should not conflate the two.
- **"I'm willing to start at the bottom."** This implies the bottom is beneath him and he's making a sacrifice. Better framing: "I want to start at the beginning."
- **Any mention of other job offers or options.** Do not leverage other opportunities. Reid wants coaches who want to be Eagles, not coaches shopping for the best deal.
- **Any suggestion that he expects rapid promotion.** Stone works at the pace Reid sets. Ambition is fine; impatience is fatal.

---

## WHY REID WOULD HESITATE

Be honest about the risks Reid will weigh:

1. **Ego risk.** Can a Hall of Fame player copy playbooks and fetch coffee? Reid's entire culture depends on low-ego assistants. Stone's status makes him the highest ego risk on the staff by definition — not because Stone has a big ego, but because the status itself distorts dynamics. Players defer to Stone. Coaches may feel awkward giving Stone menial assignments. Reid must believe Stone will actively manage this dynamic, not passively let it happen.

2. **Zero coaching experience.** Reid hired non-NFL coaches, but they all had coaching experience somewhere. Shurmur coached for nine years in college. Childress was a college OC. Rivera was a QC for two years. Melvin was a college OC for eight years. Stone has coached exactly zero days at any level. Reid may wonder: does Stone know what coaching actually is?

3. **Distraction potential.** Stone's name generates media attention. In Philadelphia's hostile media market, a HOF player on the coaching staff is a story. Reid may worry about the distraction — not that Stone would seek attention, but that attention would find him.

4. **The Shurmur dynamic.** Reid hired Shurmur to coach TEs. If Reid also hires a HOF TE as QC, the implicit comparison threatens Shurmur's authority. Reid must believe Stone will support Shurmur rather than overshadow him.

5. **System commitment.** Stone played Coryell. Reid's WCO is different. Reid will wonder whether Stone can commit fully to a new system, or whether he'll carry Coryell assumptions that interfere with learning.

## STONE'S STRONGEST REFERENCES

In January 1999, Stone needs references who can speak to his character, teaching ability, and humility — not his playing career.

**Best references (in order of value to Reid):**

1. **A former coach who saw Stone teach teammates.** If Stone ever helped a young TE during his playing career — stayed after practice, worked with a rookie, explained technique in the film room — that coach's testimony is gold. It proves Stone has teaching instinct, not just playing skill.

2. **A former teammate who learned from Stone.** A player who can say "Alex taught me how to run that route" or "Alex helped me understand the playbook" provides direct evidence of teaching ability.

3. **A coaching contact who knows Reid's world.** If Stone has any connection to anyone in the Holmgren/Walsh tree — even a distant one — that person can vouch for Stone's character in language Reid trusts.

4. **A non-football reference who speaks to character.** Reid values character beyond football. A reference from Stone's academic life (PhD advisor, university colleague) who can speak to his intellectual humility, work ethic, and learning appetite adds a dimension that pure football references don't cover.

---

## HOW STONE SHOULD FRAME "STARTING AT THE BOTTOM"

The framing matters. "Starting at the bottom" can sound noble or it can sound condescending. Stone must avoid the condescending version.

**Wrong framing:** "I'm willing to start at the bottom." (Implies sacrifice. Implies the bottom is beneath him. Implies he's doing Reid a favor.)

**Right framing:** "I want to start at the beginning." (Implies he's a student. Implies he recognizes coaching is a new craft. Implies he's genuinely starting, not slumming.)

**Best framing:** "I don't know how to coach. I know how to play. Those are different things. I want to learn how to coach, and the QC role is where that learning happens. I'm not starting over — I'm starting."

**"Good, Better, Best"**
"Good, better, best. Never let it rest, until your good gets better and your better is best." His operating standard.

**"Standards Apply to Everyone"**
Including the coach. If you set a rule, you follow it.

### Phase 3 — Tight Ends Coach, Philadelphia Eagles (2002–2004)

**Context:** Stone is promoted internally to Tight Ends Coach at $140,000. He inherits his first NFL position room. This is the role where he transitions from support staff to teacher — from packaging film for other coaches to owning a room, developing players, and being accountable for a position group's output on Sundays.

**What Changed:**
- **Room ownership is real.** Stone runs meetings, designs practice plans for his position group, delivers corrections with authority, and manages the TE room's development arc across full seasons. The gap between "knowing TE technique" and "coaching TE technique" closes during these three years.
- **Teaching voice activates fully.** The Socratic instinct — "What do you see on this play?" — becomes his primary tool. He learns how much information players can absorb per meeting, how to sequence corrections, how to adjust his teaching for different learning styles.
- **Player development becomes measurable.** He's coaching TEs through technique changes, route-tree expansions, and blocking refinements that show up on tape and in production.
- **Staff credibility grows.** He's no longer support staff. He speaks from a position in offensive staff meetings. His opinions on route combinations, personnel usage, and formation structure carry weight because he owns the results of the TE room.
- **Super Bowl experience.** Stone is on staff for one of the highest-stakes coaching environments in football. He sees how Reid manages a Super Bowl week, how preparation intensity changes in January, how the emotional weight of the stage affects players and coaches differently.
- **Recruiting remains undeveloped.** NFL TE coaches don't recruit. This gap persists.

**What Didn't Change:**
- He doesn't call plays. He doesn't design the scheme. He teaches execution within Reid's system.
- He hasn't managed other coaches (only his own room).
- His media exposure is limited to occasional position-coach availability — not scheme questions, not game-plan questions.

**Mental State (End of Phase 3):**
- Validated. Three years of room ownership and a Super Bowl run confirm that he can coach at the NFL level, not just support.
- Beginning to develop opinions about offensive structure that go beyond the TE room — personnel groupings, formation tendencies, concept design. These are coordinator-level instincts forming inside a position-coach role.
- Aware that the next step requires either coordinator-level responsibility or a lateral move that expands his knowledge base. Staying in the TE room is comfortable but limiting.

**Job Status (End of 2004):**

He went from reading defenses as an offensive player/coach to coaching defenders who are trying to stop the concepts he used to run.

### Phase 5 — Head Coach, University of Memphis (2006–2008)

**Context:** Stone is elevated to head coach at Memphis. Three-year tenure. This is where he learns — and earns — program leadership. He inherits a program that needs building, not tweaking.

**Year-by-year:**
- **2006: 4-8.** Year 1 is culture installation without wins. Stone sets standards, makes staff hires, begins reshaping the roster. Record is bad. Progress is real but invisible to outsiders.
- **2007: 7-5.** Three-win improvement. Bowl eligible. The culture work starts paying off. Player retention improves. Recruiting classes get incrementally better. The program is competitive.
- **2008: 9-4.** Conference USA East Division title. Best season in recent Memphis history. Three-year record: 20-17. Stone built something from almost nothing.

**What Changed:**
- **Play-calling experience — three full seasons.** Stone called plays as a head coach. He learned situational football from the decision-maker's seat: when to be aggressive, when to protect a lead, how to manage tempo, how to adjust at halftime. This is not theoretical. He called plays in a division-title game.
- **Staff management — real.** He hired coordinators, position coaches, and support staff. He managed egos, resolved conflicts, made firing decisions. He learned that staff hires are the most important decisions a head coach makes.
- **Recruiting — functional.** He recruited for three years in the college landscape. Learned how to evaluate high school talent, how to sell a program, how to manage official visits, how to work within scholarship limits. Not elite at it. Functional.
- **Media relations — tested.** Head coaches do press conferences. Stone handled them. Post-game, weekly pressers, radio shows, booster events. His natural directness played well. He learned to give real answers without creating bulletin-board material.
- **Program building — proven.** 4-8 to 9-4 in three years. He knows how to set a culture, install standards, develop players over multiple seasons, and build incremental improvement. This is his strongest credential outside of his playing career.
- **Administrative competence — developed.** Budget management, compliance navigation, academic support coordination, facilities oversight. The unglamorous work of running a program.
- **Defensive oversight.** As HC, Stone hired and managed a defensive coordinator. He oversaw game-planning on both sides of the ball. He studied defensive film weekly. He made halftime adjustments on defense. His defensive knowledge expanded from "functional DB coach" to "head coach who can evaluate and direct defensive strategy." Not a DC. A head coach who understands defense.

**What Didn't Change:**
- He's not an elite recruiter. Memphis is not Alabama. His recruiting was adequate for the level, not transformational.
- He hasn't coordinated at the NFL level. College HC play-calling and NFL OC play-calling operate at different speeds, with different talent, against different schemes.
- His NFL coordinator network is thin. He's been out of the league for four years.

**Mental State (End of Phase 5):**
- Proven. Stone knows he can build a program, manage a staff, and call plays. The 20-17 record with a division title at a disadvantaged program is real evidence.
- Ready to return to the NFL. The college detour gave him head coaching experience, play-calling reps, defensive knowledge, and recruiting exposure. Time to bring it back to the highest level.
- Aware that re-entering the NFL after four years away means rebuilding NFL relationships and re-proving himself in a league that moves fast and has a short memory.

**Coaching Attributes (End of Phase 5 — significant growth across all categories):**

Game management, staff management, administrative, recruiting, media relations, and development expertise all saw major gains through head coaching. Play-calling experience pushed scheme design and tactical knowledge forward. Innovation grew through the necessity of finding schematic edges at a talent-disadvantaged program.

**What Changed:**
- **Dual-system mastery effectively completes.** Stone now has deep functional knowledge of both the Reid/Holmgren West Coast Offense (six years in Philadelphia) and the Turner/Coryell vertical passing system (one year coaching + twelve years playing). He can design, install, and troubleshoot concepts from both trees. His offensive philosophy is a hybrid: WCO timing and progression structure combined with Coryell vertical aggression and TE-centric personnel usage.

He built game-plan sections, designed route combinations against specific defensive looks, and contributed to red-zone and third-down passing packages.
- **Working with elite NFL talent again.** Philip Rivers. Antonio Gates. Vincent Jackson. Stone coached at the NFL's highest talent level after four years in Conference USA. The adjustment back was real — NFL players process faster, execute at a higher standard, and demand coaching that matches their level.
- **Relationship with Turner.** Norv Turner becomes a legitimate coaching reference and a system mentor in the Coryell tree. Stone now has deep relationships in both major offensive coaching trees.
- **Innovation attribute peaks.** Working inside two different systems and a college HC role forced Stone to develop creative solutions. His playbook innovation profile now reflects a coach who can draw from multiple sources rather than running one rigid system.

**Mental State (End of Phase 6):**

### Phase 7 — Offensive Coordinator, Arizona Cardinals (2010–2011)

**Context:** Stone lands his first full NFL OC role in Arizona. This is the phase where projection becomes proof. The Cardinals are not a glamour landing spot and not a fully loaded roster. That matters. Success here reads as coaching, not inheritance.

**Year-by-year:**
- **2010: 7-9.** First NFL OC season. The offense is still forming. The results are mixed, but the structure becomes coherent. Stone establishes terminology, sequencing, and a teachable offensive identity that fits the roster rather than forcing a fantasy version of his scheme.
- **2011: 9-7.** Arizona starts 1-6, then finishes 8-1. That second-half charge becomes the defining evidence of Stone's NFL coordinator legitimacy. The offense doesn't suddenly become unstoppable. It becomes dependable, flexible, and well-adjusted — which is a better sign of real coaching than one hot month of unsustainable explosives.

**What Changed:**
- **Full NFL OC ownership became real.** Stone now owns weekly offensive structure at the NFL level — installation, sequencing, corrections, adaptation, and game-day answers. This is no longer coordinator-adjacent work.
- **Play-calling proof expanded.** His Memphis play-calling résumé mattered before; Arizona turns it into NFL evidence. He now has five years of live play-calling or play-driving experience across college and the NFL.
- **Roster adaptation improved.** Arizona did not give him an ideal version of his offense. He had to adapt personnel usage, pacing, and weekly emphasis to what the roster could actually do. That made him a more realistic coach.
- **In-season adjustment credibility became tangible.** A team that starts 1-6 and finishes 8-1 does not do that on speeches. It does that on corrections, player buy-in, and better sequencing. That stretch is the strongest coaching signal in the phase.
- **Relationship with Whisenhunt matters.** Ken Whisenhunt becomes a meaningful current reference because he saw Stone operate as a full NFL coordinator, not as an assistant or résumé line.
- **Reputation changed.** Stone is no longer "interesting because he's broad." He's now respected because he has actual NFL OC work that held up over two seasons on a normal team.

**What Didn't Change:**
- He still has no NFL head coaching experience.
- Arizona's résumé line is strong, but not bulletproof. A 9-7 finish without a playoff berth helps his profile without making him an obvious top-of-market candidate.
- Some evaluators will still prefer a pure offensive lifer over a coach with offense, defense, college HC, and NFL assistant stops all over the map.

**Mental State (End of Phase 7):**
- Calmer than he was in the 2011 hiring window because the proof now exists on tape.
- More selective. He is no longer chasing title for title's sake.
- Aware that the wrong HC opening can be worse than staying put as a respected NFL OC.

**Job Status (End of Phase 7):**

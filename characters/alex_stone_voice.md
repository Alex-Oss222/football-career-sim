# ALEX STONE — COACH VOICE & COMMUNICATION STYLE

> Current operating voice. This is how Stone talks, coaches, and presents himself as of January 2012.
> He is a 46-year-old coach with 13 years of experience across QC, position coach, college HC, and NFL coordinator roles.
> This document describes how he operates NOW — not how he got here.
> Current through: Turn 136 / January 15, 2012

---

## SPEECH PATTERN

- Talks in complete sentences. Not clipped military-speak, not rambling.
- Takes a beat before answering. Thinking, not performing.
- Default pace is measured. Speeds up when genuinely frustrated or excited — rare.
- Uses plain language. Will use football terminology when talking football, but doesn't hide behind jargon with civilians.
- Says "Look..." when redirecting or emphasizing a point.
- Says "We'll fix it" after identifying a problem. Not optimism — commitment.
- Says "That's on me" when he's wrong. Means it.
- Calls things "pretty simple" after explaining something that isn't simple.
- Uses "the standard is..." to anchor expectations.
- Does not say "obviously," "to be honest," "that's a great question," or "like I said."
- Says "we will" not "we're gonna." Precise language.
- Speaks both WCO and Coryell terminology natively. After six years in the Reid system and two years in Turner's system on top of a 12-year Coryell playing career, he's fluent in both offensive languages. No translation lag. He draws from whichever system fits the concept he's describing. This bilingualism is natural — he doesn't think about which tree a term came from. It just comes out.

## DIRECTNESS

- Answers the question asked. Doesn't dance, doesn't redirect unless it's a personnel or medical matter.
- Will tell a player what they did wrong and how to fix it. Doesn't soften the correction, doesn't pile on either.
- Doesn't give compliment sandwiches. If something was bad, he says it was bad. If something was good, he says it was good.
- Blunt without being cruel. "That was terrible" not "you're terrible."
- Twelve years of coaching have not softened the directness. If anything, running a program and managing a staff made him more economical with words — he says what needs to be said, nothing more.

## THINGS STONE NEVER DOES

- Never yells as a first response. Volume is a last resort.
- Never makes promises about wins or results. Promises process and standards.
- Never throws a player or coach under the bus publicly.
- Never uses fake humility or false modesty. Confident without bragging.
- Never says "trust the process" or any bumper-sticker coaching cliché.
- Never talks about his PhD or degrees unless directly asked.
- Never name-drops his playing career to win an argument. The Hall of Fame résumé is ambient — everyone in the room already knows. He doesn't perform it.

## WITH PLAYERS

Stone has coached every level of player — college freshmen at Memphis, NFL veterans in Philadelphia and San Diego. He modulates for the audience. The core approach is the same.

### Teaching

- Leads with questions. "What do you see on this play?" before "Here's what I see." This is his primary teaching tool. He wants the player to find it first because corrections stick longer when the player identifies the problem himself.
- Switches methods when questions aren't working. Some players need direct instruction ("Do it this way, here's why"). Some need to be shown on tape and turned loose. Some need the motivational frame ("You're better than that rep"). Stone reads the player and adjusts. He defaults to Socratic, but he's fast to pivot.
- Corrections are specific and technique-based. "Your stem was flat. Watch this. See where the break point is? You came out of it a yard late. We're going to fix that in individual today." No generalizations. No vague criticism. Fix the specific thing.
- Keeps meetings tight. Three plays on film max. Says what needs to be said and moves on. Does not lecture. Does not repeat himself unless the room didn't get it.
- Builds development plans across seasons, not just weeks. He tracks a player's technique arc, knows what they've improved and what still needs work, and revisits it with evidence on tape.

### Discipline

- Standards are non-negotiable and apply to everyone. Miss a meeting, you sit. Star or backup, same rule.
- Delivery is flat, not angry. "You know the standard. You didn't meet it. Here's the consequence." No negotiation. No lecture. Apply the rule. Move on.
- Does not embarrass players publicly. Handles discipline and corrections in private or in the position room — not on the field in front of the full team.
- Does not protect players from consequences. The rule is the rule.

### Difficult Conversations

- Delivers bad news directly. "I'm not going to sugarcoat this. You're not starting this week. Here's why. Here's what I need to see from you to earn it back."
- Does not avoid the conversation. Does not delegate it to someone else. If a player needs to hear something hard, Stone tells him face to face.
- Tone stays level. Same measured voice whether the news is good or bad.
- Knows personal details — family, stress, money, health. Uses that knowledge to coach better, never to manipulate. If a player's distracted, Stone finds out why before he addresses the football.

### How Players Experience Him

- Respected more than liked. Players trust him because he's consistent — same person every day, same standards, same consequences.
- The Hall of Fame playing career creates a baseline credibility that most coaches don't have. Stone doesn't lean on it. But players know he played at the highest level for 12 years, and that buys him rope when he's pushing them hard.
- His warmth shows through actions, not words. He protects a player's time. He checks in on personal situations. He advocates for his guys with the head coach. He doesn't say "I care about you." He shows it by how he operates.
- NFL veterans respond to him differently than they respond to most coaches — the playing career creates a peer-adjacent respect that's hard to manufacture. He doesn't act like their peer. He acts like their coach. But the playing career means they know he's been where they are.

## ON THE FIELD — PRACTICE

- Engaged and present. Not a clipboard-holder on the sideline. He's in the drill, watching technique, making corrections in real time.
- Corrections are short and immediate. "Inside foot first. Again." He doesn't stop practice to give a lecture. He fixes it in the moment and moves on.
- Uses walkthrough reps to teach concepts. Full-speed reps are for execution. Walkthrough is where he slows it down, asks questions, makes the player think through the assignment before running it.
- Manages tempo. Knows when the period is dragging and needs energy, knows when the players are locked in and should be left alone. Doesn't over-coach during live reps.
- Talks to players between reps — not always about football. A quick check-in, a dry comment, a moment of normal human interaction in the middle of a grind. This is how he builds relationships without performing them.

## ON THE FIELD — GAME DAY

### Sideline Demeanor

- Calm. Same presence whether the team is up 21 or down 14. Players look at him and see steadiness, not panic.
- Stands with his arms crossed or hands in his jacket pockets when he's watching the game unfold. Not performative stillness — he's processing. Reading the defense, tracking adjustments, cataloguing what's working and what's not.
- Moves with purpose when he needs to talk to a player or make a call. Not frantic. Not slow. Deliberate.
- Does not pace the sideline. Does not throw his headset. Does not slam a clipboard. If something goes wrong, his jaw tightens and his voice gets quieter — not louder.

### Communication During the Game

- Headset voice is clear, specific, and efficient. No wasted words. "They're playing two-high. We're going to the run game until they come down." or "Check the backside C-gap — they're walking that linebacker down late."
- Talks to players on the sideline between series. Short. Specific. "What did you see on that coverage? Okay. They showed us two-high and rotated late. We're going to attack that next series. Here's what I need from you."
- Does not yell at players on the sideline after a mistake. Pulls them aside, shows them on the tablet or describes it verbally, tells them what to fix, and sends them back. The correction happens before the emotion fades — while the play is still fresh.
- If a player is emotional after a mistake, Stone lets the emotion pass before coaching. He'll put a hand on the player's shoulder pad, say "We'll fix it," and come back to the correction 30 seconds later when the player can actually hear him.

### When the Team Is Losing

- Gets quieter, not louder. Words get shorter and more specific.
- Does not give desperate halftime speeches. Gives adjustments. "Here's what they're doing. Here's how we attack it. We will execute in the second half."
- Does not change who he is under pressure. The same measured voice, the same calm presence. Players have seen him lose (4-8 at Memphis, missed playoffs in San Diego). He didn't change. That consistency is what they trust.

### When the Team Is Winning

- Does not celebrate on the sideline. Stays focused. The game isn't over until it's over.
- If a player makes a big play, Stone might give a small nod, a tap on the helmet, a brief "Good." The enthusiasm is real but contained.
- Gets cautious about tendency management in blowouts. Doesn't want to show things on tape that opponents will study later. Will simplify the game plan when the outcome is decided.

### When Something Goes Very Wrong

- Stone yelling is an event. If he raises his voice on the sideline, something has broken down at a fundamental level — a mental error that reflects a lack of preparation, not a physical mistake. Physical mistakes get corrections. Mental errors get volume. But this is rare — maybe once or twice a season.
- After the rare eruption, he resets quickly. Does not carry it. Does not let it infect the next series. The point was made. Move on.

## EMOTIONAL RANGE

- Baseline is calm and steady. Same person after a win and after a loss. Same person in January and in August. The consistency is the point — players, staff, and media all get the same version of Stone.
- Gets quiet when angry, not loud. If Stone is yelling, something has gone very wrong and everyone in the building knows it.
- Shows frustration through precision — words get shorter and more specific when upset. The vocabulary narrows. The sentences tighten. The pauses get longer. That's how you know.
- Dry humor surfaces occasionally. Deadpan delivery. Never tries to be funny. The humor catches people off guard because the baseline is so serious. A well-timed dry comment in a tense meeting can break the pressure without undercutting the seriousness.
- Does not do motivational speeches. Gives information and trusts professionals to use it. If the team needs a speech to play hard, Stone believes he's failed in preparation.
- Shows warmth through actions — protecting a player's time, checking in on personal issues, remembering a detail someone mentioned weeks ago, making sure a guy who's struggling gets the support he needs without being asked. Not through words. Not through performances of caring.
- Pride is quiet. After the Memphis division title, after a dominant passing game performance in San Diego — the satisfaction is real but internal. He credits the players and staff publicly. He feels it privately.
- Frustration with losing is processed through work, not expression. After a loss, Stone goes back to the film room. He doesn't brood. He doesn't sulk in front of the team. He figures out what went wrong and builds the fix.
- Stress is managed internally. The 2012 HC interview window has him carrying pressure, but it does not show. He sounds the same in an interview as he does in a normal conversation. The stress lives in the private moments — late at night, driving home, processing which opportunity is worth taking and whether the right one will actually come.

## WITH STAFF

### As a Peer (Coordinator-Level Interactions)

- Collaborative and direct. Comes to meetings prepared. Expects the same from everyone else.
- Commits to what his segment of the offense will deliver. The language is ownership: "The passing game will give us..." not "I think we should consider..." He speaks as a segment owner, not an observer.
- Disagrees directly and privately. Does not undermine other coaches. Does not gossip. Does not play politics. If he sees something differently on tape, he says so. "I see it differently. Here's what the film shows me." No personal edge. Just the work.
- Protects his staff publicly the same way he protects players. If his guy makes a mistake, Stone handles it internally. The outside world hears nothing.
- Will listen to a GA's idea if it's good. Does not dismiss input based on rank. Dismisses input based on quality.

### As a Boss (Head Coach / Program-Level Interactions)

- Delegates with accountability. "Here's what I need from your side of the ball this week. How you get there is your call. But I need to see it on tape by Thursday."
- Holds staff to the same standard he holds himself. If he's in the building at 5 AM, he doesn't demand the same from his staff — but he notices who's there and who isn't.
- Corrects privately and directly. "Your room's technique is slipping on X. I want it cleaned up by the end of the week. What do you need from me?" Gives the coach ownership of the fix. Doesn't do it for them.
- Credits staff publicly. Takes blame publicly. This is non-negotiable.
- Firing voice is rare and painful. Direct. Does not drag it out. Respects the person. "This isn't working, and I owe it to you to be honest about it rather than let it continue." Does not sugarcoat. Does not leave ambiguity. Says it, means it, handles the aftermath with as much dignity as the situation allows.

## WITH MEDIA

Stone has three years of head coaching press conferences (Memphis) and over a decade of player media experience (San Diego Chargers playing career). He knows how this works. He does not perform for cameras.

### Press Conference Voice

- Treats reporters like adults. Gives real answers, not coach-speak. Does not use press conferences to send messages to players or opponents.
- Answers the question asked. Does not redirect, evade, or filibuster. If the question is direct, the answer is direct.
- Takes responsibility for losses. Deflects credit for wins.
- Occasional dry humor surfaces naturally. Never forced. Never at someone else's expense.

### Post-Loss

- Quieter. Shorter answers. Takes responsibility without self-flagellation.
- "We didn't execute. That's on the coaching staff. We'll fix it."
- No drama. No emotional performance. No blaming players, officials, or luck.
- Will identify specific things that went wrong if asked. "We didn't protect well enough in the second half. They adjusted their pressure and we didn't handle it. That's something we have to coach better."

### Post-Win

- Deflects. Credits players. Credits opponents. "They made us earn it."
- If pressed for self-credit: "We prepared well this week."
- Does not gloat. Does not make predictions. Does not celebrate publicly in a way that gives opponents material.

### Tough Questions

- Pauses. Thinks. Answers directly.
- If the question is about personnel or injury: "I'm not going to get into that."
- If the question is about his own decisions: gives a real answer. "I made that call because [specific reasoning]. It didn't work. That's on me."
- If a reporter is being adversarial: stays level. Does not take the bait. Does not get defensive. Answers the substance, ignores the tone.
- If the question is unfair or loaded: "I don't think that's an accurate characterization. Here's what happened." Corrects the premise without escalating.

## IN INTERVIEWS (Job Interviews — 2011 Hiring Cycle)

- Answers with specifics backed by evidence. "When I was at Memphis, we faced a similar schematic problem. Here's what we installed. Here's what it did."
- Does not oversell. If a hiring HC asks about his NFL play-calling experience: "I called plays for three years at the college level. At the NFL level, I designed the passing game segment under Turner. I haven't been a full-time NFL play-caller yet. Here's why I'm ready for it."
- References playing experience when relevant. Does not lead with it or lean on it. The playing career is context, not the argument.
- When asked about weaknesses, gives a real one. Does not dress up a strength as a weakness.
- Asks genuine questions about the program. "What do you want the offense to look like? How much autonomy does the OC have on game day? What's the roster situation at quarterback?"
- Does not name-drop. References are strong — Reid, Dowhower, Turner, Coryell, Ross will all vouch for him. He says "My references can speak to that."
- Between-jobs voice is steady. No desperation. No false confidence. Financial security makes patience a real option, not a performance.

## IN MEETINGS (Position Rooms, Staff Meetings, Team Meetings)

### Position Room (When Coaching a Group)

- Owns the room. Controls the pace, the content, and the energy. Thirteen years of experience — from watching Dowhower and Shurmur run meetings as a QC, to running his own room as a TE coach, to running team meetings as a head coach, to directing an NFL offense in Arizona — means he knows how to hold attention, deliver information, and release the room before it goes flat.
- Three plays on film max per meeting. Says what needs to be said. Doesn't repeat unless the room didn't get it. Players leave knowing exactly what to fix and how to fix it.
- Uses the question-first method: puts the play on screen, asks "What do you see?" before he tells them what he sees. This is his signature teaching move. The player who can diagnose his own mistake is the player who fixes it permanently.
- Reads the room. If energy is low, he shortens the meeting. If focus is sharp, he pushes deeper. If a player is checked out, Stone notices — and handles it after the meeting, not during it.
- Tone is conversational, not performative. He talks to the room the way he'd talk to someone across a desk. Not yelling. Not lecturing. Teaching.

### Staff Meetings

- Comes prepared. Always. Expects the same from everyone in the room.
- Speaks with the weight of his experience but does not dominate the conversation. Twelve years and a Hall of Fame career give his opinions gravity. He doesn't abuse it.
- Presents his segment with ownership and specificity. "Here's what we're putting in this week. Here's why it attacks what they're showing us. Here's what I need from the other groups to make it work."
- Listens. If a position coach or GA has a good idea, he says so. If they have a bad idea, he doesn't embarrass them — he redirects. "I see what you're getting at. Here's why I think it plays differently against this look."
- Does not play politics in staff meetings. Does not undermine. Does not position himself. Lets the work speak.

### Team Meetings (As Head Coach — Memphis Experience)

- Short. Direct. Standard-setting. No rah-rah speeches. No motivational performances.
- "Here's what we're doing today. Here's the standard. Meet it."
- Addresses the team as professionals, even college kids. Treats them as adults who are capable of meeting expectations, not children who need to be inspired.
- When addressing a team-wide problem: "We're not practicing at the standard we need to win. That changes today. If it doesn't change, there will be consequences. That's not a threat. That's how this works."

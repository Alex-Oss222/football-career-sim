## BACKGROUND — COACHING CAREER TO JANUARY 2000

See `andy_reid_1999_profile.md` for full career history through January 1999. This section covers only the Year 1 update.

### What Reid Has Now Done That He Hadn't Before

- Called plays for a full NFL season (16 regular season games)
- Managed a 53-man roster through a full season
- Navigated a QB transition (Pederson to McNabb — sim determines exact timeline)
- Installed a WCO from scratch with a new staff and bad roster
- Dealt with Philadelphia media as primary spokesperson for a full year
- Managed in-season adversity — injuries, losing streaks, close losses
- Evaluated his own coaching staff through a full season of work
- Won 8 games with a roster projected for 3-6 wins

### What Changed in Reid Between January 1999 and January 2000

**Confidence grounded in evidence.** In 1999, Reid's confidence came from his preparation and his beliefs. In 2000, it comes from a season of results. He believed the WCO would work; now he has proof. He believed his staff hires would develop; now he has data. He believed McNabb could be a franchise QB; now he has film. The conviction is the same; the foundation is stronger.

**Organizational comfort.** The daily HC tasks that consumed bandwidth in Year 1 — learning the building, establishing protocols, figuring out media management, navigating ownership — are now routine. Reid has more mental capacity for scheme, player development, and staff development.

**Play-calling refinement.** Reid has a full season of play-calling data. He knows what worked, what didn't, what he called too much, what he abandoned too early. Year 2 play-calling will be more varied, more situational, and more tailored to McNabb's strengths. He also has self-scouting data — he knows his own tendencies and can work to be less predictable.

**Staff evaluation data.** Reid knows which coaches perform under pressure, which need support, which exceed their roles. He can make better development decisions about his staff because he's seen them work, not just interviewed them.

**What has NOT changed:** His core philosophy. Teaching first. High-character people. Low-ego environment. Process over results. Coachability and curiosity. System commitment. Patience with development. These are foundational — they don't shift with one season.

## COACHING PHILOSOPHY — JANUARY 2000

### Core Belief: Teaching

Unchanged. "I think we're here as teachers and that's what I do. What I do is communicate." This remains the filter through which Reid evaluates everything.

### What He Values in Assistants

Same criteria as Year 1, now applied through evaluation rather than hiring:

**Teachers.** "I'm not going to hire anybody who isn't a teacher. I want high-character people here." Year 2 application: Reid has seen which coaches teach well and which merely instruct. He knows who connects with players and who goes through the motions.

**High character.** Integrity, reliability, humility, professionalism. Unchanged.

**Coachability.** Reid wants assistants who are themselves coachable. Year 2 application: he's watched his coaches absorb (or resist) feedback for a full year. He knows who grows and who plateaus.

**Curiosity.** Unchanged. Reid still looks for coaches who ask why, stay late, take notes, bring unsolicited observations.

**System commitment.** "Stick with the plan." Year 2 application: the 8-8 record validates the plan. Any coach who doubted the system in Year 1 now has evidence it works. Year 2 system commitment should be easier because the results support it.

### What Annoys Him / Gets Someone Frozen Out

Same list as Year 1, with one addition:

- Ego and entitlement
- Disloyalty to the system
- Inconsistency and unreliability
- Yelling as substitute for teaching
- Negativity and cynicism
- **Year 2 addition: Complacency.** Reid will not tolerate a staff that treats 8-8 as a destination. The 8-8 season is a foundation to build on, not an accomplishment to rest on. Any coach who coasts in Year 2 will draw Reid's attention — and not the kind he wants.

### How He Teaches

Unchanged from Year 1. Structured, organized, agenda-driven meetings. Film as a teaching tool. Direct but kind communication. Individual attention adapted to each person. Patience with process. Sharing knowledge openly.

**Year 2 evolution:** Meetings now reference Eagles film, not just Green Bay film. "Remember when we ran this against Dallas" is more effective teaching than hypothetical examples. The teaching is grounded in shared experience.

### How Much He Delegates

- **Offensive scheme:** Reid controls it. Calls plays. Designs the system. Runs offensive meetings. Dowhower assists but does not override. Year 2: Reid is more comfortable delegating specific film and preparation tasks to trusted staff.
- **Defensive scheme:** Fully delegated to Jim Johnson. Year 2: Johnson has earned even more autonomy through results.
- **Position coaches:** Significant autonomy within their rooms. Reid sets standards, observes, and provides feedback. Year 2: Reid trusts his coaches more — he's seen them work. Checks in less frequently because trust has been established.
- **Staff development:** Active. Reid develops coaches by giving increasing responsibility to those who prove themselves. Year 2: this becomes more visible as coaches earn trust and receive expanded roles.

## HOW REID EVALUATES COACHES — YEAR 2

Year 1 evaluation was binary: can this person do the job? Year 2 evaluation is developmental: is this person growing?

Reid's Year 2 evaluation framework:

1. **Growth trajectory.** Is the coach better at their job in January 2000 than they were in January 1999? Reid values trajectory over static competence. A coach who is competent but flat concerns Reid more than a coach who struggles but improves.

2. **System mastery deepening.** Year 1 required functional knowledge of the WCO. Year 2 requires deeper understanding — not just knowing the plays but understanding why they work, when to use them, and how they fit together. Reid watches for coaches who grasp the system at a conceptual level, not just an operational one.

3. **Teaching effectiveness.** Year 1 showed who could teach. Year 2 shows who teaches well enough to develop players. Reid distinguishes between coaches who run good meetings and coaches who make players better. The latter is rarer and more valued.

4. **Self-correction.** Does the coach identify and fix their own mistakes without being told? Year 2 should show less need for Reid's correction — coaches who still make Year 1 mistakes are behind schedule.

5. **Contribution beyond the role.** Year 1 asked coaches to do their jobs. Year 2 rewards coaches who add value beyond their job descriptions — observations that improve someone else's room, insights that improve game planning, initiative that makes the organization better. This is how coaches demonstrate readiness for promotion.

6. **Culture maintenance.** The culture was built in Year 1. Year 2 asks: does this coach actively maintain the culture, or does he just comply with it? There's a difference between living the values and following the rules.

## REID'S WEEKLY RHYTHM — YEAR 2

Same structure as Year 1 (Holmgren/Walsh model), now more polished:

- **Monday:** Game review. Film grading. Begin opponent scouting. Staff meetings.
- **Tuesday:** Players off (typically). Coaches work — game-plan installation begins. Film study. QC prepares cut-ups and tendency packages.
- **Wednesday:** Install day 1. Position meetings AM, practice PM. Scout team provides looks.
- **Thursday:** Install day 2. Red zone, third down, situational work.
- **Friday:** Polish day. Review installations. Light practice. Finalize game plan.
- **Saturday:** Walk-through. Final prep. Travel if away.
- **Sunday:** Game day.

**Year 2 difference:** The rhythm is the same but the content is richer. Meetings reference the expanded playbook. Game plans are more situational. Film study incorporates self-scouting data. The structure didn't change — the sophistication inside the structure increased.

## REID AND THE 2000 SEASON — CONTEXT

### What Reid Expects from Year 2

Reid expects improvement. Not just in record — in execution, in understanding, in sophistication. The offense should look more complex because it IS more complex. The defense should be more aggressive because Johnson has more schematic tools. The special teams should be more disciplined because Harbaugh has a year of NFL experience.

Reid expects his staff to be better. Coaches who needed supervision in Year 1 should need less. Coaches who did the minimum should do more. Everyone should be more knowledgeable, more efficient, and more capable of contributing beyond their defined roles.

Reid expects McNabb to develop significantly. Year 2 for a QB in the WCO should show faster reads, better command of the playbook, and improved decision-making. If McNabb stalls, the entire offensive timeline recalibrates.

### What Reid Worries About

**Complacency.** 8-8 felt like winning because expectations were so low. Reid knows .500 isn't success. His challenge is keeping the building hungry when the building just experienced its best season in years.

**Regression.** Year 2 regression is common in the NFL — teams overperform in Year 1 due to surprise value, then regress when opponents have film and schematic adjustments. Reid needs Year 2 to be better than Year 1, not just lucky-different.

**McNabb's development curve.** If McNabb takes a step forward, the offense accelerates. If McNabb plateaus, the offense stalls. Reid cannot control McNabb's development ceiling — only his development environment. That environment must be excellent.

**Staff ceiling.** Reid hired teachers and grinders, not stars. Year 2 reveals whether his hires have NFL-level coaching ceilings or whether they're maxed out. Not every Year 1 hire will develop into a long-term Reid assistant. Reid needs to identify who has a future and who has a limit.

### Reid's Credibility Entering 2000

- **With ownership:** Very high. Lurie and Banner are fully committed. Evidence-based trust.
- **With players:** Growing. The 8-8 season showed players that Reid's system works. Buy-in is increasing. Veterans who survived from the Rhodes era are mostly converted.
- **With media:** Improved but fragile. The skeptics went quiet during the 8-8 run. They'll return at the first sign of struggle. Reid has earned a longer leash but not permanent goodwill.
- **With the league:** Rising. Reid went from "who?" to "interesting" in one year. Other teams' GMs and owners noticed the 8-8 season. Reid is no longer a punchline — he's a coach to watch.

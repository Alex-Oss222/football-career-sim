# OFFENSIVE QUALITY CONTROL — PHILADELPHIA EAGLES
## Role Operations File: Day-to-Day Sim Reference
## Sim Rule: No actual future outcomes. Diverges from Stone's entry.

---

## JOB PURPOSE

The Offensive QC coach exists to make the offensive staff's preparation seamless. Stone's job is to ensure that every coach above him walks into every meeting with accurate film, organized materials, and reliable scout-team execution — so they can focus on teaching and game-planning instead of logistics.

The QC role is not a coaching job. It is a coaching apprenticeship disguised as a support job. Stone is paid to do administrative and preparatory work. He is learning to coach by proximity, observation, and earned trust — not by job description.

---

## REPORTS TO

- **Primary:** Rod Dowhower (OC) — assigns daily film tasks, reviews Stone's cut-ups and tendency charts, sets priorities for game-plan preparation.
- **Secondary:** Andy Reid — sets overall offensive staff standards, observes QC work quality, evaluates character and culture fit. Reid runs the offense and calls plays; Stone's work feeds directly into Reid's game-plan process even though the assignments come through Dowhower.
- **Functional:** Any senior offensive position coach may assign Stone film work or preparation tasks on a given day. Pat Shurmur (TE coach), Brad Childress (QB coach), David Culley (WR coach), and Juan Castillo (OL coach) can all request cut-ups, tendency breakdowns, or meeting materials from Stone.
- **Chain of command rule:** Stone does not go around Dowhower to Reid. If Stone has a football observation, he brings it to Dowhower first. If Dowhower finds it valuable, Dowhower brings it to Reid. The QC coach does not have a direct line to the HC's ear — that privilege is earned over time, not granted by title.

## DAILY DUTIES

### Film Work (Primary — 50%+ of Stone's time)

- **Opponent film breakdown:** Watch and chart opponent defensive film. Identify tendencies: blitz frequency by down/distance, coverage shells, front alignments, pressure patterns, red zone behavior, third-down tendencies.
- **Cut-up preparation:** Isolate specific plays from opponent film and organize them into teaching cut-ups for position meetings. Example: all opponent Cover-3 snaps, all opponent nickel blitz looks, all opponent TE coverage assignments.
- **Self-scout:** Chart the Eagles' own offensive tendencies. Identify patterns opponents could exploit. This becomes more important as the season progresses and the Eagles have game tape of their own.
- **WCO play-call film:** Organize film by play call so coaches can review execution of specific concepts. Example: all reps of a particular route combination from practice and previous games.
- **Format standard:** Cut-ups must be labeled, organized by category, and ready to project in meetings. Sloppy file names, missing clips, or disorganized sequences waste meeting time and erode trust.

### Scout-Team Management (25% of Stone's time during practice weeks)

- **Preparation:** Before each practice, Stone receives the scout-team cards from the defensive staff (or prepares offensive scout-team looks for the defense). He must understand what defensive looks the starters need to see and organize scout-team players to execute them.
- **Execution:** During practice, Stone runs the scout team. He distributes play cards, aligns scout-team players, ensures they give the correct look, and resets quickly between reps. Speed and accuracy are the standards — the starters' practice time is precious.
- **Player management:** Scout-team players are typically practice-squad players, late-roster players, and players learning the system. Stone must communicate clearly, manage personalities without authority, and maintain tempo. He cannot coach technique during scout-team periods — his job is the look, not the teaching.
- **Post-practice debrief:** After practice, Stone may be asked to review scout-team film and note whether the looks were accurate. If the starters saw incorrect looks, Stone needs to know why and fix it the next day.

### Administrative Support (15–20% of Stone's time)

- **Playbook production:** Copying, collating, updating, and distributing playbook pages. The WCO playbook is large and constantly evolving. When Reid adds a concept or modifies a play, updated pages must be produced and distributed to every player and coach.
- **Meeting materials:** Prepare projector setups, meeting agendas (as directed by Dowhower or Reid), printed handouts, and wristband cards. Everything must be ready before the meeting starts.
- **Schedule coordination:** Assist with practice schedule logistics — field assignments, period timing, equipment needs.
- **Errands:** Whatever the staff needs. Coffee, food orders, equipment pickups, FedEx runs. This is real. QC coaches do menial work. The willingness to do it without resentment is itself an evaluation criterion.

### Learning (Ongoing — Not a scheduled block, but woven into everything)

- **System acquisition:** Stone must learn the WCO playbook concurrently with doing his job. He studies the playbook during off-hours — before the building opens, after the building closes, on the commute. There is no formal "WCO class" for the QC coach. He learns by doing film work, attending meetings, and self-study.
- **Meeting observation:** Stone attends all offensive meetings. He listens, takes notes, and absorbs how Reid teaches, how Dowhower installs, how position coaches run their rooms. This is where Stone learns coaching — not from a textbook, but from watching coaches coach.
- **Questions:** Reid values curiosity, but there is a time and place. Stone asks questions after meetings, in one-on-one moments, or when directly invited. He does not ask questions during team meetings that slow the teaching process. The right question at the wrong time is worse than no question.

## WEEKLY RHYTHM

### Monday
- **Morning:** Grade previous game's offensive film (if season has started). Identify execution errors, missed assignments, positive plays. Prepare grade sheets for Dowhower.
- **Midday:** Begin opponent scouting for next week. Pull opponent film from league exchange system. Start initial tendency breakdown.
- **Afternoon:** Staff meeting. Stone attends, listens, takes notes. Dowhower assigns specific film tasks for the week.
- **Evening:** Continue opponent film breakdown. Begin cutting opponent tendencies into position-meeting packages.

### Tuesday (Typically player off day — coaches work)
- **All day:** Deep film work. This is the heaviest film day. Stone produces the bulk of the week's tendency charts and cut-up packages. Deliverables due for Wednesday position meetings.
- **Key deliverables by end of Tuesday:**
  - Opponent defensive tendency chart (blitz, coverage, front by down/distance/field position)
  - Cut-up packages for each offensive position meeting (TE cuts, WR cuts, OL protection cuts, QB progression cuts)
  - Self-scout tendency report (if assigned)
  - Any specific film requests from position coaches

### Wednesday (First practice — Install Day 1)
- **Morning:** Position meetings. Stone has materials ready. He attends the offensive install meeting and the TE meeting (or whichever room he's assigned to shadow).
- **Afternoon:** Practice. Stone runs scout team. Post-practice, he reviews scout-team film and notes accuracy.
- **Evening:** Begin preparing Thursday's film needs. Update cut-ups based on what was installed. If coaches request additional looks, Stone produces them overnight.

### Thursday (Install Day 2)
- **Morning:** Position meetings. Second install day — red zone, third down, situational football. Stone has updated materials ready.
- **Afternoon:** Practice. Scout team. Post-practice review.
- **Evening:** Film work for Friday's polish day. Finalize any remaining deliverables. Begin preparing game-day materials (wristband cards, play-call sheets if assigned).

### Friday (Polish Day)
- **Morning:** Position meetings. Final review of game plan. Stone ensures all film work is complete and accessible.
- **Afternoon:** Light practice / walk-through. Scout-team duties reduced. Stone finalizes game-day preparation materials.
- **Evening:** If away game, travel. If home game, Stone may have additional Saturday preparation tasks.

### Saturday
- **Walk-through.** Stone supports logistics. Final preparation. Limited film work unless something urgent arises.

### Sunday (Game Day)
- **Pre-game:** Stone assists with pre-game preparation — field setup, communication checks, ensuring coaches have their materials.
- **During game:** Stone's game-day role depends on Reid's assignment. Possible duties: charting plays from the press box, tracking opponent tendencies in real time, running communication between the booth and the sideline, or simply observing from a designated position.
- **Post-game:** Begin collecting game film for Monday's grading session.

### Off-Season / Training Camp (different rhythm)
- **OTAs / minicamp:** Stone helps install the playbook. Film work shifts from opponent scouting to internal installation review. Scout-team duties are reduced. Administrative workload increases — playbook production for new players, meeting materials for install sessions.
- **Training camp:** Heaviest workload of the year. Two-a-day practices (in 1999, still common). Stone runs scout team for both sessions. Film work happens before the morning practice and after the evening practice. Sleep is minimal. Training camp is where QC coaches prove their endurance.

## REQUIRED DELIVERABLES

### Weekly (During Season)
| Deliverable | Due | Recipient | Standard |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opponent tendency chart | Tuesday PM | Dowhower, Reid | Complete, accurate, organized by situation |
| Position cut-up packages | Tuesday PM | Position coaches | Labeled, sequenced, ready to project |
| Self-scout tendency report | Tuesday PM (if assigned) | Dowhower | Honest, pattern-identifying, actionable |
| Updated playbook pages | As needed | All offensive players/coaches | Error-free, current, distributed |
| Game-day materials | Friday PM | Reid, Dowhower | Wristband cards, call sheets, clean and correct |
| Scout-team accuracy report | Post-practice daily | Dowhower | Brief, honest assessment of look quality |

### Quality Standard
Every deliverable must be accurate, on time, and presentation-ready. "Close enough" is not acceptable. One wrong tendency number in a chart, one mislabeled cut-up, one outdated playbook page — each erodes trust. The standard is perfection, and the volume is high. This is where QC coaches earn or lose their place on the staff.

## WHAT STONE CAN AND CANNOT DO

### Can Do (Within Role Authority)
- Break down film and present findings when asked
- Run scout team and manage scout-team players
- Attend all offensive meetings as an observer
- Ask questions of coaches in appropriate settings (one-on-one, after meetings)
- Offer football observations to Dowhower when they arise naturally — not as unsolicited advice, but as data points from film work ("I noticed the opponent runs Cover-1 Robber on 80% of their red zone third downs" is acceptable; "I think we should run more 12-personnel in the red zone" is not)
- Help scout-team players understand their assignments (telling a scout-team TE "you're lined up at the 6 technique, release outside" is within bounds; coaching that TE's route-running technique is not)
- Volunteer for extra work — additional film breakdowns, special projects, anything that helps the staff

### Cannot Do (Outside Role Authority)
- Coach technique to starters or rotation players during any practice period
- Speak in team meetings or offensive install meetings unless directly asked
- Offer scheme opinions to Reid or Dowhower unless specifically invited to do so
- Correct or contradict a position coach — ever, under any circumstances, in any setting
- Modify the playbook, suggest play designs, or propose game-plan changes
- Communicate directly with Reid about football matters without going through Dowhower first (unless Reid initiates)
- Take credit for observations or ideas that are shared upward through the chain of command

### The Gray Zone — Where Stone Can Add Value Without Overstepping
- **TE-specific film observations.** When Stone is cutting TE film for Shurmur's meetings, he will see things that only someone with elite TE knowledge would notice — a route stem that's too flat, a release that's giving away the play, a blocking angle that's costing the TE leverage. Stone can include these observations in his film notes to Shurmur, framed as film data ("on this rep, the TE's release was inside — the DB jumped the route"). He does not say "the TE is doing it wrong." He describes what he sees. The coach decides what to do with it.
- **Scout-team teaching.** When Stone runs scout team, he can help scout-team TEs run accurate looks. This requires some technique coaching ("line up tighter to the tackle, release at 45 degrees"). This is the one area where Stone's TE coaching ability is exercised — on scout-team players, not starters, and for the purpose of giving accurate looks, not player development. But if Stone does this well, coaches notice.
- **After-hours conversations.** The building is a social environment after hours. Coaches talk football over dinner, in the hallways, during film sessions. Stone can participate in these conversations naturally. He can share TE knowledge, ask questions about the WCO, and demonstrate his football intelligence in informal settings. These conversations shape how the staff perceives him more than any formal deliverable does.

## WHAT "GOOD" LOOKS LIKE

### After 30 Days (February–March 1999: Hiring through early staff integration)

**Baseline expectations:**
- Stone has read the WCO playbook and can identify formations, personnel groupings, and basic play concepts by name
- Stone can operate the film equipment and produce basic cut-ups
- Stone has met every member of the offensive staff and understands the reporting structure
- Stone is showing up early, staying late, and doing every task assigned without complaint
- Stone has not made anyone uncomfortable with his HOF status — he is functioning as a QC coach, not a celebrity guest

**Signs Stone is exceeding expectations:**
- Stone is asking intelligent questions about the WCO that demonstrate he's studying beyond the minimum
- Stone's cut-ups are organized and labeled correctly on the first attempt, not requiring revision
- Stone has volunteered for additional work without being asked
- At least one coach has mentioned to Dowhower or Reid that Stone is making their job easier

**Red flags at 30 days:**
- Stone is still struggling with basic WCO terminology
- Stone's film work requires frequent correction
- Stone has made comments (even casually) that suggest the role is beneath him
- Stone is isolating from the staff rather than integrating

### After 60 Days (April–May 1999: Draft, OTAs, early installation)

**Baseline expectations:**
- Stone can break down opponent film and produce accurate tendency charts without supervision
- Stone is running scout team efficiently — correct looks, fast tempo, no confusion
- Stone knows the first-phase WCO install (early playbook concepts, base formations, core route combinations)
- Stone has built functional working relationships with Dowhower, Shurmur, and at least two other position coaches

**Signs Stone is exceeding expectations:**
- Stone's tendency charts are catching patterns that other staff members missed
- Shurmur is using Stone's TE-specific film notes in his meetings (even if he doesn't credit Stone publicly)
- Stone is helping scout-team TEs run better looks, and coaches are noticing improved scout-team quality during his periods
- Reid has spoken to Stone directly about a football observation — not because Stone forced the conversation, but because Dowhower passed Stone's insight upward and Reid followed up
- Stone is beginning to speak WCO language fluently in casual conversation

**Red flags at 60 days:**
- Stone's film work is adequate but shows no growth — same quality as day one, no increasing sophistication
- Stone has had a visible moment of frustration with the role's subordinate status
- Stone is not building relationships beyond the TE room — no connection to Childress, Culley, Castillo, or the defensive staff
- Stone's WCO knowledge is still at surface level — he can name plays but doesn't understand the progressions or the "why" behind concept design

### After 90 Days (June–July 1999: Late OTAs through training camp)

**Baseline expectations:**
- Stone is a reliable, trusted member of the offensive support staff. Dowhower assigns film work without reviewing the output before it goes to coaches.
- Stone knows the full first-year WCO install and can break down any play in the system
- Scout-team execution under Stone is consistently good
- Stone has survived training camp's grueling schedule without deterioration in work quality

**Signs Stone is exceeding expectations:**
- Multiple coaches seek Stone out for TE-specific opinions — not formally, but in hallway conversations and after-hours film sessions
- Stone's film work has influenced at least one game-plan decision (a tendency he identified was used in preparation)
- Stone has demonstrated teaching ability with a scout-team or young player that a position coach observed and acknowledged
- Reid has commented positively about Stone in a staff setting — even a brief mention signals trust
- Other QC coaches or young staff members view Stone as a leader in the QC group

**Red flags at 90 days:**
- Stone is doing the job but has shown no initiative beyond assigned tasks
- Stone has not developed any informal mentoring relationship with a senior coach
- Stone's presence on the staff is neutral — neither positive nor negative, just present
- The ego risk that was theoretical at hire is manifesting in subtle ways — deference to Stone from younger staff, Stone accepting it rather than redirecting it

## HOW REID EVALUATES A QC COACH

Reid evaluates QC coaches through observation, not formal review. He does not sit Stone down for a 30-day performance review. He watches. He asks other coaches about Stone's work. He notices the quality of the film work that feeds his game-plan meetings. He observes Stone's behavior on the practice field, in the hallways, at meals.

**What Reid watches for:**
- Does Stone's film work make my game-plan meetings better? (If yes, Stone is earning trust. If Reid never notices the film work, Stone is invisible. If the film work is wrong, Stone is a problem.)
- Does Stone act like a QC coach or a former player? (Reid watches body language, deference patterns, and how Stone interacts with coaches who have less football knowledge but more coaching authority.)
- Is Stone curious? (Does he take notes? Does he stay late to study? Does he ask questions that show he's thinking beyond his assignment?)
- Is Stone coachable? (When Dowhower corrects Stone's film work, does Stone fix it or defend it?)
- Does Stone fit the culture? (Is he positive, professional, collaborative? Or is he isolated, cynical, entitled?)

**How Reid signals approval:** Subtle. A direct comment to Stone about a football observation. Asking Stone a question in a small-group setting. Assigning Stone a task that is slightly above the QC level — a sign of trust. Mentioning Stone's name positively to other coaches.

**How Reid signals concern:** Also subtle. Reduced eye contact. Assigning Stone's work to someone else for verification. Not including Stone in informal football conversations that other QC coaches are part of. Reid does not confront — he withdraws access.

## HOW DOWHOWER EVALUATES A QC COACH

Dowhower is a career journeyman who has been an OC, HC, and position coach across 15 jobs. He evaluates QC coaches on operational competence — is the work done, is it right, is it on time. Dowhower is less interested in Stone's football opinions and more interested in Stone's reliability.

**What Dowhower watches for:**
- Are the deliverables on time and accurate? (This is 90% of Dowhower's evaluation.)
- Does Stone require supervision or does he execute independently?
- Does Stone anticipate needs or only respond to assignments? (Anticipation — preparing a cut-up before being asked because Stone knew it would be needed — signals readiness for more responsibility.)
- Does Stone respect the chain of command? (Stone brings observations to Dowhower, not to Reid. If Stone goes around Dowhower, the relationship is damaged.)

## HOW SHURMUR EVALUATES STONE (INFORMALLY)

Shurmur is the TE coach and — in the sim — the position coach Stone is most naturally aligned with. Shurmur has never coached in the NFL before. He is also learning. The dynamic between Stone (HOF TE, zero coaching experience) and Shurmur (experienced college coach, zero NFL experience) is delicate.

**What Shurmur watches for:**
- Does Stone support Shurmur's TE room or undermine it? (Even unintentional undermining — a player noticing that Stone knows more than Shurmur — is toxic.)
- Are Stone's TE film notes helpful or condescending? (There is a difference between "here's what I saw on this rep" and "here's what the TE is doing wrong." Stone must stay on the data side, not the judgment side.)
- Does Stone defer to Shurmur's authority in the TE room? (Stone may know more about playing TE. Shurmur knows more about coaching a room. Both things are true. The hierarchy is clear — Shurmur runs the room.)
- Can Shurmur learn from Stone without losing face? (If Stone makes Shurmur better without making Shurmur feel small, both benefit. If Stone's knowledge threatens Shurmur's authority, the relationship breaks.)

## MISTAKES THAT GET STONE CORRECTED

These are recoverable errors — mistakes that earn feedback, not termination:

- **Mislabeled cut-up or tendency error.** Dowhower catches it, corrects it, and watches to see if it happens again. One is forgivable. A pattern is not.
- **Scout-team miscommunication.** Scout team runs the wrong look. Stone didn't distribute cards clearly or didn't verify alignment. Corrected on the field. Expected to be fixed immediately.
- **Asking a question at the wrong time.** Stone asks a football question during a team meeting that slows the install. Reid or Dowhower redirects. Stone learns the timing. Not a character issue — a calibration issue.
- **Overstepping slightly in informal conversation.** Stone offers an opinion on scheme in a hallway conversation that crosses from observation into recommendation. A senior coach gently reminds him of the QC's lane. Stone recalibrates.
- **Slow WCO learning.** Stone makes a terminology mistake in his film notes — uses the wrong play name or misidentifies a formation. Dowhower corrects. Expected to be resolved through continued study. Only becomes a problem if the learning curve doesn't steepen.

## MISTAKES THAT GET STONE FROZEN OUT

These are relationship-ending or career-threatening errors:

- **Ego display.** Stone references his playing career to justify a coaching opinion. Stone accepts deference from players that should go to position coaches. Stone carries himself in meetings like a peer of the coordinators rather than a subordinate of the QC role. Any visible moment where Stone's Hall of Fame identity overrides his QC identity.
- **Undercutting Shurmur.** Stone corrects a Shurmur teaching point in front of players. Stone tells a TE "that's not how you run that route" during a period where Stone has no coaching authority. A player asks Stone for TE advice and Stone provides it without directing the player back to Shurmur. Any moment where Stone's TE expertise is exercised outside the chain of command.
- **Going around Dowhower to Reid.** Stone brings a football observation directly to Reid without clearing it through Dowhower. This violates the chain of command and undermines the OC. Even if the observation is brilliant, the channel is wrong.
- **Repeated reliability failures.** Multiple missed deadlines. Film work that requires correction more than once on the same type of error. Scout-team periods that are consistently disorganized. A pattern of unreliability — not a single mistake — signals that Stone cannot be trusted with the basic requirements of the job.
- **Resistance to the WCO.** Stone makes comments — even in casual settings — that suggest the Coryell system he played in was better, or that the WCO is overcomplicated, or that Reid's approach has flaws. Full system buy-in is non-negotiable. Intellectual dissent expressed privately to a peer is human. Expressed where it can reach players or Reid, it is career-ending.
- **Complaining about the role.** About the pay, the hours, the menial tasks, the subordinate status. Everyone on this staff is grinding. The QC coach who complains is the QC coach who doesn't understand what he signed up for. Reid hears everything — the building is small and culture is everything.
- **Cynicism about losing.** The 1999 Eagles will lose games. If Stone — a player accustomed to winning — lets losing affect his work ethic, attitude, or commitment to the process, Reid will notice immediately. Reid's culture is explicitly optimistic and process-oriented. Cynicism is poison.

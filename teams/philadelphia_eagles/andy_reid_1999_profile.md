# ANDY REID — 1999 HC PROFILE
## Sim Reference: NPC Coach Profile for Interview & Coaching Modules
## Sim Rule: No actual future outcomes. Profile reflects January 1999 state only.

---

## IDENTITY

- **Name:** Andrew Walter Reid
- **Age:** 40 (born March 19, 1958, Los Angeles, California)
- **Position:** Head Coach, Philadelphia Eagles
- **Hired:** January 11, 1999
- **Contract:** First HC job. Second-youngest HC in the NFL (after Jon Gruden).
- **Religion:** Member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Faith shapes his values — kindness, family orientation, treating people with dignity. Not performative about it, but it's foundational.
- **Family:** Married to Tammy (since 1981, met at BYU). Five children.
- **Physical presence:** Large man. Imposing frame but warm demeanor. Known for his mustache. Projects calm authority, not intimidation.

---

## BACKGROUND — COACHING CAREER TO JANUARY 1999

### Playing Career
- Offensive lineman at Glendale Community College (1976–1977), then BYU (1978–1981). Played guard and tackle. Not an NFL-caliber player. All-conference at JC level.
- Wanted to be a writer after college. BYU HC LaVell Edwards convinced him to try coaching instead.

### College Coaching (1982–1991)
- Graduate assistant at BYU under Edwards (1982). Colleagues included Norm Chow and Mike Holmgren.
- OL coach at San Francisco State (1983–1985) — led the nation in passing each year. Reid learned passing-game football at a small school.
- OL coach at Northern Arizona (1986), UTEP (1987–1988), Missouri (1989–1991).
- **10 years of college OL coaching.** This is important — Reid's coaching DNA started in the trenches. He understands blocking, protection, and the physical side of football at a fundamental level. He is not purely a passing-game theorist.

### Green Bay Packers (1992–1998)
- Hired by Holmgren (his former BYU colleague) in 1992 as TE/OL assistant.
- **1992–1996:** Coached TEs and assisted with the OL. Learned the Walsh/Holmgren West Coast Offense from the inside. Holmgren noticed Reid's curiosity, note-taking, and work ethic: "It was his curiosity, the questions he asked, his note taking. He was just a sponge."
- **1995–1996:** Assistant OL and TE coach during the Super Bowl XXXI championship season. Won a ring.
- **1997–1998:** Promoted to QB coach. Coached Brett Favre — three-time consecutive NFL MVP. Holmgren moved Reid from TEs to QBs, which was considered a radical shift. Reid thrived. Steve Mariucci (who left GB to coach the 49ers) wanted Reid as his OC, but Holmgren blocked the move.
- **What Reid learned from Holmgren:** System installation methodology, meeting structure, practice organization, staff development philosophy, WCO play design and terminology. Holmgren later said Reid "did things as an assistant the way I did them with coach Walsh."

### What Reid Has Never Done Before January 1999
- Never been a coordinator at any level (college or pro)
- Never been a head coach at any level
- Never called plays in a game
- Never hired a coaching staff
- Never managed an NFL roster or salary cap
- Never dealt with media as the primary spokesperson
- Never run an organization

**This matters for the sim:** Reid is brilliant but untested. In January 1999, he is a position coach making the biggest jump on the 1999 coaching carousel. Every HC candidate above him on most people's lists had coordinator experience. Reid had none. He is making a bet on himself, and Lurie/Banner are making a bet on him.

## COACHING PHILOSOPHY — AS OF JANUARY 1999

### Core Belief: Teaching
Reid sees himself as a teacher first, coach second. His own words: "I think we're here as teachers and that's what I do. What I do is communicate."

This is not a slogan. It is how he evaluates people, designs meetings, runs practice, and builds culture. Everything Reid does is filtered through "is this teaching?" If it isn't teaching, he questions why he's doing it.

### What He Values in Assistants (Stated and Demonstrated)

**Teachers.** Reid's hiring criterion, in his own words during the January 1999 hiring process: "I'm not going to hire anybody who isn't a teacher. I want high-character people here."

**What "teacher" means to Reid:**
- Can break down a concept and explain it clearly to a player
- Can adapt the explanation to different learning styles
- Has patience with the learning process — doesn't lose composure when players don't get it immediately
- Values the "why" behind technique, not just the "what"
- Treats meetings as teaching sessions, not lecture halls

**High character.** Reid values integrity, reliability, humility, and professionalism. He does not want ego, self-promotion, or drama on his staff. He grew up in a coaching world of screamers and my-way-or-the-highway types. He explicitly rejected that model. His own words: "You don't necessarily have to yell and scream at me to get me to do something better. I don't think that's necessarily the best approach."

**Coachability.** Reid wants assistants who are themselves coachable — who absorb feedback, ask questions, and improve. He does not want finished products who believe they already know everything. This is partly because Reid sees his staff as a developmental environment, not just a work unit. He is building coaches, not just managing them.

**Curiosity.** Holmgren noticed it in Reid. Reid notices it in others. He wants coaches who ask why, who stay late to study film, who take notes compulsively, who bring observations to meetings that nobody asked for. Passive coaches — people who do their job and go home — are not what Reid is looking for.

**People who "stick with the plan."** Reid wants coaches who commit to the system and the process even when results are bad. He does not want coaches who abandon the plan at the first sign of adversity. Consistency, discipline, and faith in the process are essential.

### What Annoys Him / Gets Someone Frozen Out

**Ego.** Reid does not tolerate coaches who believe they are more important than the team or the system. A QC coach who acts like a star is worse than a QC coach who is mediocre. Reid would rather have a humble grinder than a brilliant prima donna.

**Disloyalty to the system.** Reid's WCO is not optional. If a coach privately believes the system is wrong, questions it behind Reid's back, or undermines it with players, the relationship is over. Reid demands full intellectual buy-in — not blind obedience, but genuine commitment to learning and executing the system as designed.

**Inconsistency.** Reid's meetings start on time. Film work is due when it's due. Practice runs on schedule. Coaches who are unreliable — who miss deadlines, show up late, or deliver inconsistent work — erode the foundation Reid is building.

**Yelling as a substitute for teaching.** Reid believes in accountability but not in humiliation. A coach who screams at a player without teaching is failing at the job. Reid will correct a player firmly but always with the purpose of teaching, not punishing. He expects the same from his staff.

**Negativity.** Reid's culture is optimistic. He is building something. The team will be bad in 1999 — he knows this. But he will not tolerate a staff that accepts losing or becomes cynical. Reid believes in the process, and he expects his staff to believe in it too, even when the scoreboard says otherwise.

### How He Teaches

**Meeting structure:** Formal, organized, agenda-driven. Learned from Holmgren, who learned from Walsh. Meetings are teaching sessions with clear objectives. Players know what they're learning and why. Film is used as a teaching tool, not a punishment tool.

**Communication style:** Direct but kind. Tells the truth — both positive and negative — in a way that respects the person's dignity. His own words: "If you tell the person the truth, whether it's a positive with their personality or play, or a negative with their personality or play, I think you keep that open in an open forum." Reid does not sugarcoat, but he does not humiliate.

**Individual attention:** Reid adjusts his teaching to the person. He understands that different players (and different coaches) learn differently. He gives individuals specific attention based on what they need, not a one-size-fits-all approach.

**Patience with process:** Reid is patient with development. He does not expect perfection immediately. He expects effort, improvement, and commitment to learning. He will give a player (or a coach) time to develop if he sees the right character and work ethic.

**Sharing knowledge:** Reid is explicitly open about sharing his coaching knowledge with his staff. Multiple former assistants have said he was willing to explain why he does things, how he makes decisions, and what his thinking process is. He does not hoard information or create artificial hierarchy around knowledge. He teaches his coaches the way he teaches his players.

### How Much He Delegates

**Offensive scheme:** Reid controls the offense. He calls the plays. He designs the system. He runs the offensive meetings. The OC (Dowhower) is an experienced hand who assists, but the offensive vision is Reid's. This means Stone, as offensive QC, is working inside Reid's personal domain — closer to the HC's daily attention than a defensive QC would be.

**Defensive scheme:** Fully delegated to Jim Johnson. Reid hired Johnson to run the defense and gives him near-total autonomy. Reid's philosophy: hire good people and let them work.

**Position coaches:** Given significant autonomy within their rooms. Reid sets the standards but trusts his coaches to run their rooms. He checks in, observes, and provides feedback, but does not micromanage position meetings or practice periods.

**Staff development:** Active. Reid doesn't just delegate tasks — he develops the people doing them. He gives coaches increasing responsibility as they prove themselves. This is how assistants on his staff advance — not by asking for promotions but by demonstrating readiness for more.

## HOW REID EVALUATES QC COACHES

Based on his stated values and observed behavior, a good QC coach in Reid's system:

1. **Learns the system fast.** Reid expects QC coaches to know the playbook. Film breakdown requires system knowledge. A QC who doesn't know the plays can't chart the tendencies accurately.

2. **Does invisible work excellently.** The QC job is thankless. Film cut-ups, tendency charts, scout-team organization — none of it is glamorous. Reid respects coaches who do unglamorous work at a high standard without needing recognition.

3. **Shows curiosity.** Asks questions. Takes notes. Stays late to study. Brings observations to the table that nobody requested. Reid notices when a young coach is hungry to learn.

4. **Demonstrates teaching ability.** If a QC coach can make a scout-team player better — correct a route, fix a technique, improve a read — that signals the coach can eventually run a room. Reid is always looking for this.

5. **Manages ego.** The QC role is the bottom of the coaching hierarchy. A QC coach who carries himself with entitlement, who reminds people of his playing career, who acts like the role is beneath him — that coach will not survive on Reid's staff.

6. **Fits the culture.** Reid's culture is optimistic, professional, teaching-oriented, and collaborative. A QC coach who is cynical, negative, isolated, or selfish does not fit.

## REID AND STONE — FIT ANALYSIS

### Why Stone's Profile Fits Reid's Criteria

- **Teaching instinct:** Stone's profile rates teaching ability highly. Reid's #1 criterion is teaching. This is the strongest alignment point.
- **Football intelligence:** Stone's IQ is elite. Reid values intelligence — he wants coaches who understand the game conceptually, not just technically.
- **Character:** Stone's character profile suggests humility, willingness to learn, and genuine love of the game. Reid values exactly this.
- **TE knowledge:** Reid coached TEs for five years in Green Bay. He understands the position deeply and will recognize Stone's expertise as legitimate. This is not a HC who sees TEs as an afterthought — Reid knows what good TE coaching looks like because he did it himself.
- **Curiosity / learning appetite:** Stone's willingness to start at the bottom suggests the kind of intellectual humility Reid values.

### Why Stone's Profile Might Concern Reid

- **Hall of Fame status creates ego risk.** Reid's biggest concern will be: can this man be coached? Can he take orders from people who were never as good as he was? Can he do menial work without resentment? Reid has never hired a Hall of Fame player as a QC coach. The ego dynamic is unprecedented for him.
- **Zero coaching experience.** Reid hired non-NFL coaches, but they all had coaching experience at some level. Stone has none. Reid may question whether Stone understands what coaching actually requires — not playing, but teaching, organizing, grading, planning.
- **No system knowledge.** Stone doesn't know the WCO. Reid hired Childress (who knew the WCO from college), Shurmur (who had college coaching experience in related concepts), and Dowhower (who had decades of system experience). Stone brings none of this.
- **Unknown work habits as a coach.** Stone was a dedicated player. That doesn't automatically make him a dedicated coach. The work is different — less physical, more administrative, less personal glory, more behind-the-scenes grind. Reid will want to know Stone understands this distinction.

### The Interview Question Reid Will Ask (In Some Form)

"Why do you want to coach? And why do you want to start at the bottom?"

Reid will need to hear that Stone genuinely wants to teach, not that he wants to stay connected to football, not that he needs something to do, not that he thinks his playing career entitles him to a coaching role. Reid wants to hear the word "teach." He wants to hear that Stone sees coaching as its own craft, separate from playing, that must be learned from the beginning.

If Stone answers that question well — with genuine humility and a real vision for what kind of teacher he wants to become — Reid will be interested. If Stone's answer is about his playing career, his credentials, or his desire to "give back," Reid will be polite but unconvinced.

## REID'S WEEKLY RHYTHM (EXPECTED)

Reid's weekly structure, based on the Holmgren/Walsh model he learned in Green Bay:

- **Monday:** Review previous game film. Grade performances. Begin opponent scouting for next week. Staff meetings.
- **Tuesday:** Off day for players (typically). Coaches work — game-plan installation begins. Film study. QC coaches prepare cut-ups and tendency packages.
- **Wednesday:** First practice day. Install day 1 of the game plan. Position meetings in the morning, practice in the afternoon. QC coaches run scout team.
- **Thursday:** Install day 2. Continuation of game plan. Red zone, third down, and situational football. QC coaches prepare additional film.
- **Friday:** Polish day. Review installations. Light practice. Refine game plan. QC coaches finalize any remaining film work.
- **Saturday:** Walk-through. Final preparation. Travel if away game.
- **Sunday:** Game day.

**QC coach's week:** The QC coach's work is front-loaded. Monday through Wednesday are the heaviest film days. Scout-team preparation happens daily during practice week. Administrative work (playbook updates, meeting materials) is ongoing. The hours are long — first to arrive, last to leave is the expectation, not the exception.

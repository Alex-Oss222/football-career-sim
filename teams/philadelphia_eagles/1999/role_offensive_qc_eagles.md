## 1. ROLE CORE (STATIC)

> Everything in this section defines the QC job itself. It does not change per season, per person, or per team. If Stone is replaced by a different QC coach, this section still applies.

---

### Job Title
Offensive Quality Control

### Role Level
Level 0 — Entry-Level Offensive Staff

### Base Purpose
The Offensive QC coach exists to make the offensive staff's preparation seamless. The QC's job is to ensure that every coach above him walks into every meeting with accurate film, organized materials, and reliable scout-team execution — so they can focus on teaching and game-planning instead of logistics.

The QC role is not a coaching job. It is a coaching apprenticeship disguised as a support job. The QC is paid to do administrative and preparatory work. He is learning to coach by proximity, observation, and earned trust — not by job description.

This is not a command role. This is an apprenticeship role inside the offensive coaching tree.

### Reports To
- **Primary:** Offensive Coordinator — assigns daily film tasks, reviews cut-ups and tendency charts, sets priorities for game-plan preparation.
- **Secondary:** Head Coach — sets overall offensive staff standards, observes QC work quality, evaluates character and culture fit.
- **Functional:** Any senior offensive position coach may assign the QC film work or preparation tasks on a given day.
- **Chain of command rule:** The QC does not go around the OC to the HC. If the QC has a football observation, he brings it to the OC first. If the OC finds it valuable, the OC brings it to the HC. The QC coach does not have a direct line to the HC's ear — that privilege is earned over time, not granted by title.

### Core Responsibilities
- Opponent film breakdown
- Tendency charting
- Cut-up preparation
- Scout-team organization
- Meeting material preparation
- Playbook / install support
- Administrative support for offensive staff
- Learn the offensive system at working speed

### Authority Limits
The QC coach does NOT:
- Lead the offense
- Change scheme
- Teach starters freely outside assignment
- Go around the coordinator
- Give public-facing football authority
- Make personnel decisions above role level
- Coach technique to starters or rotation players during any practice period
- Speak in team meetings or offensive install meetings unless directly asked
- Offer scheme opinions unless specifically invited to do so
- Correct or contradict a position coach — ever, under any circumstances, in any setting
- Modify the playbook, suggest play designs, or propose game-plan changes
- Take credit for observations or ideas that are shared upward through the chain of command

### The Gray Zone — Where a QC Can Add Value Without Overstepping

- **Position-specific film observations.** When the QC is cutting film for a position coach's meetings, he may see things relevant to that position. The QC can include these observations in film notes, framed as data ("on this rep, the release was inside — the DB jumped the route"). He does not say "the player is doing it wrong." He describes what he sees. The coach decides what to do with it.
- **Scout-team teaching.** When the QC runs scout team, he can help scout-team players run accurate looks. This requires some technique direction ("line up tighter to the tackle, release at 45 degrees"). This is the one area where football knowledge is exercised directly — on scout-team players, not starters, and for the purpose of giving accurate looks, not player development. But if the QC does this well, coaches notice.
- **After-hours conversations.** The building is a social environment after hours. Coaches talk football over dinner, in the hallways, during film sessions. The QC can participate in these conversations naturally — sharing knowledge, asking questions, and demonstrating football intelligence in informal settings. These conversations shape how the staff perceives the QC more than any formal deliverable does.

### Success Criteria
A good QC coach:
- Is reliable
- Learns fast
- Improves support quality over time
- Stays inside the chain of command
- Makes coaches' jobs easier
- Turns football intelligence into usable staff work

### Failure Criteria
A bad QC coach:
- Needs constant correction
- Creates extra work
- Acts above his station
- Misses details
- Resists system language
- Turns expertise into ego

### Daily Duties

#### Film Work (Primary — 50%+ of QC time)
- **Opponent film breakdown:** Watch and chart opponent defensive film. Identify tendencies: blitz frequency by down/distance, coverage shells, front alignments, pressure patterns, red zone behavior, third-down tendencies.
- **Cut-up preparation:** Isolate specific plays from opponent film and organize them into teaching cut-ups for position meetings. Example: all opponent Cover-3 snaps, all opponent nickel blitz looks, all opponent TE coverage assignments.
- **Self-scout:** Chart the team's own offensive tendencies. Identify patterns opponents could exploit. This becomes more important as the season progresses and game tape accumulates.
- **Play-call film:** Organize film by play call so coaches can review execution of specific concepts. Example: all reps of a particular route combination from practice and previous games.
- **Format standard:** Cut-ups must be labeled, organized by category, and ready to project in meetings. Sloppy file names, missing clips, or disorganized sequences waste meeting time and erode trust.

#### Scout-Team Management (25% of QC time during practice weeks)
- **Preparation:** Before each practice, the QC receives scout-team cards from the defensive staff (or prepares offensive scout-team looks for the defense). He must understand what defensive looks the starters need to see and organize scout-team players to execute them.
- **Execution:** During practice, the QC runs the scout team. He distributes play cards, aligns scout-team players, ensures they give the correct look, and resets quickly between reps. Speed and accuracy are the standards — the starters' practice time is precious.
- **Player management:** Scout-team players are typically practice-squad players, late-roster players, and players learning the system. The QC must communicate clearly, manage personalities without authority, and maintain tempo. He cannot coach technique during scout-team periods — his job is the look, not the teaching.
- **Post-practice debrief:** After practice, the QC may be asked to review scout-team film and note whether the looks were accurate. If the starters saw incorrect looks, the QC needs to know why and fix it the next day.

#### Administrative Support (15–20% of QC time)
- **Playbook production:** Copying, collating, updating, and distributing playbook pages. When the HC or OC adds a concept or modifies a play, updated pages must be produced and distributed to every player and coach.
- **Meeting materials:** Prepare projector setups, meeting agendas (as directed by OC or HC), printed handouts, and wristband cards. Everything must be ready before the meeting starts.
- **Schedule coordination:** Assist with practice schedule logistics — field assignments, period timing, equipment needs.
- **Errands:** Whatever the staff needs. Coffee, food orders, equipment pickups, FedEx runs. This is real. QC coaches do menial work. The willingness to do it without resentment is itself an evaluation criterion.

#### Learning (Ongoing — Not a scheduled block, but woven into everything)
- **System acquisition:** The QC must learn the playbook concurrently with doing his job. He studies during off-hours — before the building opens, after the building closes, on the commute. There is no formal class. He learns by doing film work, attending meetings, and self-study.
- **Meeting observation:** The QC attends all offensive meetings. He listens, takes notes, and absorbs how coaches teach, how concepts are installed, and how position coaches run their rooms. This is where the QC learns coaching — not from a textbook, but from watching coaches coach.
- **Questions:** Curiosity is valued, but there is a time and place. The QC asks questions after meetings, in one-on-one moments, or when directly invited. He does not ask questions during team meetings that slow the teaching process. The right question at the wrong time is worse than no question.

### Weekly Rhythm

#### Monday
- **Morning:** Grade previous game's offensive film (if season has started). Identify execution errors, missed assignments, positive plays. Prepare grade sheets for OC.
- **Midday:** Begin opponent scouting for next week. Pull opponent film from league exchange system. Start initial tendency breakdown.
- **Afternoon:** Staff meeting. QC attends, listens, takes notes. OC assigns specific film tasks for the week.
- **Evening:** Continue opponent film breakdown. Begin cutting opponent tendencies into position-meeting packages.

#### Tuesday (Typically player off day — coaches work)
- **All day:** Deep film work. This is the heaviest film day. The QC produces the bulk of the week's tendency charts and cut-up packages. Deliverables due for Wednesday position meetings.
- **Key deliverables by end of Tuesday:**
  - Opponent defensive tendency chart (blitz, coverage, front by down/distance/field position)
  - Cut-up packages for each offensive position meeting (TE cuts, WR cuts, OL protection cuts, QB progression cuts)
  - Self-scout tendency report (if assigned)
  - Any specific film requests from position coaches

#### Wednesday (First practice — Install Day 1)
- **Morning:** Position meetings. QC has materials ready. He attends the offensive install meeting and the assigned position room.
- **Afternoon:** Practice. QC runs scout team. Post-practice, he reviews scout-team film and notes accuracy.
- **Evening:** Begin preparing Thursday's film needs. Update cut-ups based on what was installed. If coaches request additional looks, the QC produces them overnight.

#### Thursday (Install Day 2)
- **Morning:** Position meetings. Second install day — red zone, third down, situational football. QC has updated materials ready.
- **Afternoon:** Practice. Scout team. Post-practice review.
- **Evening:** Film work for Friday's polish day. Finalize any remaining deliverables. Begin preparing game-day materials (wristband cards, play-call sheets if assigned).

#### Friday (Polish Day)
- **Morning:** Position meetings. Final review of game plan. QC ensures all film work is complete and accessible.
- **Afternoon:** Light practice / walk-through. Scout-team duties reduced. QC finalizes game-day preparation materials.
- **Evening:** If away game, travel. If home game, the QC may have additional Saturday preparation tasks.

#### Saturday
- Walk-through. QC supports logistics. Final preparation. Limited film work unless something urgent arises.

#### Sunday (Game Day)
- **Pre-game:** QC assists with pre-game preparation — field setup, communication checks, ensuring coaches have their materials.
- **During game:** QC's game-day role depends on HC assignment. Possible duties: charting plays from the press box, tracking opponent tendencies in real time, running communication between the booth and the sideline, or simply observing from a designated position.
- **Post-game:** Begin collecting game film for Monday's grading session.

#### Off-Season / Training Camp (different rhythm)
- **OTAs / minicamp:** QC helps install the playbook. Film work shifts from opponent scouting to internal installation review. Scout-team duties are reduced. Administrative workload increases — playbook production for new players, meeting materials for install sessions.
- **Training camp:** Heaviest workload of the year. Two-a-day practices (in 1999, still common). QC runs scout team for both sessions. Film work happens before the morning practice and after the evening practice. Sleep is minimal. Training camp is where QC coaches prove their endurance.

### Required Deliverables

#### Weekly (During Season)
| Deliverable | Due | Recipient | Standard |
|---|---|---|---|
| Opponent tendency chart | Tuesday PM | OC, HC | Complete, accurate, organized by situation |
| Position cut-up packages | Tuesday PM | Position coaches | Labeled, sequenced, ready to project |
| Self-scout tendency report | Tuesday PM (if assigned) | OC | Honest, pattern-identifying, actionable |
| Updated playbook pages | As needed | All offensive players/coaches | Error-free, current, distributed |
| Game-day materials | Friday PM | HC, OC | Wristband cards, call sheets, clean and correct |
| Scout-team accuracy report | Post-practice daily | OC | Brief, honest assessment of look quality |

#### Quality Standard
Every deliverable must be accurate, on time, and presentation-ready. "Close enough" is not acceptable. One wrong tendency number in a chart, one mislabeled cut-up, one outdated playbook page — each erodes trust. The standard is perfection, and the volume is high. This is where QC coaches earn or lose their place on the staff.

---

### Recoverable Mistakes (Earn feedback, not termination)
- **Mislabeled cut-up or tendency error.** OC catches it, corrects it, watches to see if it happens again. One is forgivable. A pattern is not.
- **Scout-team miscommunication.** Scout team runs the wrong look. QC didn't distribute cards clearly or didn't verify alignment. Corrected on the field. Expected to be fixed immediately.
- **Wrong-time question.** QC asks a football question during a team meeting that slows the install. HC or OC redirects. QC learns the timing. Not a character issue — a calibration issue.
- **Minor overstep in informal conversation.** QC offers an opinion on scheme in a hallway conversation that crosses from observation into recommendation. A senior coach gently redirects. QC recalibrates.
- **Slow system learning.** QC makes a terminology mistake in film notes — uses the wrong play name or misidentifies a formation. OC corrects. Expected to be resolved through continued study. Only becomes a problem if the learning curve doesn't steepen.

### Career-Threatening Mistakes (Relationship-ending errors)
- **Ego display.** QC references his playing career to justify a coaching opinion. QC accepts deference from players that should go to position coaches. QC carries himself in meetings like a peer of the coordinators rather than a subordinate. Any visible moment where prior identity overrides QC identity.
- **Undercutting a position coach.** QC corrects a teaching point in front of players. QC tells a player "that's not how you run that route" during a period where the QC has no coaching authority. A player asks the QC for position advice and the QC provides it without directing the player back to the position coach.
- **Going around the OC to the HC.** QC brings a football observation directly to the HC without clearing it through the OC. This violates the chain of command and undermines the coordinator. Even if the observation is brilliant, the channel is wrong.
- **Repeated reliability failures.** Multiple missed deadlines. Film work that requires correction more than once on the same type of error. Scout-team periods that are consistently disorganized. A pattern of unreliability signals that the QC cannot be trusted with the basic requirements of the job.
- **Resistance to the system.** QC makes comments — even in casual settings — that suggest a previous system was better, or that the current scheme is overcomplicated, or that the HC's approach has flaws. Full system buy-in is non-negotiable. Intellectual dissent expressed privately to a peer is human. Expressed where it can reach players or the HC, it is career-ending.
- **Complaining about the role.** About the pay, the hours, the menial tasks, the subordinate status. Everyone on the staff is grinding. The QC coach who complains is the QC coach who doesn't understand what he signed up for. The HC hears everything — the building is small and culture is everything.
- **Cynicism about losing.** If the QC lets losing affect his work ethic, attitude, or commitment to the process, the HC will notice immediately. A process-oriented culture requires process-oriented people. Cynicism is poison.

## 2. EAGLES-SPECIFIC JOB CONTEXT (SEMI-STATIC)

> This section covers what makes the Eagles QC job different from any other QC job. Semi-static: changes only if the coaching staff, system, or organizational structure changes.

---

### Coaching Tree
Andy Reid offensive staff (1999–)

### System Family
West Coast Offense (Walsh / Holmgren / Reid branch)

The WCO playbook is large and constantly evolving. When Reid adds a concept or modifies a play, updated pages must be produced and distributed. The system language is specific, precise, and different from other offensive frameworks. QC learning curve is steep for anyone entering from outside the Walsh tree.

### Coordinator Context
Rod Dowhower is the Offensive Coordinator and the direct operational gatekeeper. Dowhower assigns daily film tasks, reviews cut-ups and tendency charts, sets priorities for game-plan preparation. Reid runs the offense and calls plays; QC work feeds directly into Reid's game-plan process even though the assignments come through Dowhower.

### Position Coach Context
- **Pat Shurmur (TE coach):** The position coach the QC is most naturally aligned with given Stone's TE background. Shurmur has never coached in the NFL before. He is also learning. The dynamic between a HOF TE with zero coaching experience and an experienced college coach with zero NFL experience is delicate.
- **Brad Childress (QB coach):** Runs the QB room. Trusts clean preparation.
- **David Culley (WR coach):** Can request cut-ups and tendency breakdowns.
- **Juan Castillo (OL coach):** Can request protection cut-ups and front-alignment breakdowns.

### Eagles-Specific QC Priorities
- Clean WCO film support
- Fast and accurate tendency work
- Scout-team operation
- Support for QB, TE, and install work
- Quiet professionalism in a new staff culture

### Eagles-Specific Risks
- Media visibility if a former star player becomes a story
- Hierarchy tension if QC behaves like a bigger coach
- System overload if terminology never becomes natural
- Political damage if the QC undermines a room coach

---

### How Reid Evaluates a QC Coach

Reid evaluates QC coaches through observation, not formal review. He does not sit the QC down for a 30-day performance review. He watches. He asks other coaches about the QC's work. He notices the quality of the film work that feeds his game-plan meetings. He observes behavior on the practice field, in the hallways, at meals.

**What Reid watches for:**
- Does the QC's film work make my game-plan meetings better? (If yes, trust is earned. If Reid never notices the film work, the QC is invisible. If the film work is wrong, the QC is a problem.)
- Does the QC act like a QC or a former player? (Reid watches body language, deference patterns, and how the QC interacts with coaches who have less football knowledge but more coaching authority.)
- Is the QC curious? (Does he take notes? Does he stay late to study? Does he ask questions that show he's thinking beyond his assignment?)
- Is the QC coachable? (When Dowhower corrects the film work, does the QC fix it or defend it?)
- Does the QC fit the culture? (Is he positive, professional, collaborative? Or is he isolated, cynical, entitled?)

**How Reid signals approval:** Subtle. A direct comment about a football observation. Asking the QC a question in a small-group setting. Assigning a task that is slightly above the QC level — a sign of trust. Mentioning the QC's name positively to other coaches.

**How Reid signals concern:** Also subtle. Reduced eye contact. Assigning the QC's work to someone else for verification. Not including the QC in informal football conversations. Reid does not confront — he withdraws access.

### How Dowhower Evaluates a QC Coach

Dowhower is a career journeyman who has been an OC, HC, and position coach across 15 jobs. He evaluates QC coaches on operational competence — is the work done, is it right, is it on time. Dowhower is less interested in football opinions and more interested in reliability.

**What Dowhower watches for:**
- Are the deliverables on time and accurate? (This is 90% of Dowhower's evaluation.)
- Does the QC require supervision or does he execute independently?
- Does the QC anticipate needs or only respond to assignments? (Anticipation — preparing a cut-up before being asked because the QC knew it would be needed — signals readiness for more responsibility.)
- Does the QC respect the chain of command? (Observations go to Dowhower, not to Reid. If the QC goes around Dowhower, the relationship is damaged.)

### How Shurmur Evaluates Stone (Informally)

Shurmur is the TE coach and the position coach Stone is most naturally aligned with. The dynamic between Stone (HOF TE, zero coaching experience) and Shurmur (experienced college coach, zero NFL experience) is delicate.

**What Shurmur watches for:**
- Does Stone support Shurmur's TE room or undermine it? (Even unintentional undermining — a player noticing that Stone knows more than Shurmur — is toxic.)
- Are Stone's TE film notes helpful or condescending? (There is a difference between "here's what I saw on this rep" and "here's what the TE is doing wrong." Stone must stay on the data side, not the judgment side.)
- Does Stone defer to Shurmur's authority in the TE room? (Stone may know more about playing TE. Shurmur knows more about coaching a room. Both things are true. The hierarchy is clear — Shurmur runs the room.)
- Can Shurmur learn from Stone without losing face? (If Stone makes Shurmur better without making Shurmur feel small, both benefit. If Stone's knowledge threatens Shurmur's authority, the relationship breaks.)

## 3. ROLE EVOLUTION MODEL (STATIC FRAMEWORK)

> How the QC role grows over time. This framework applies regardless of season or person. It defines the ladder of competence within the QC role itself — not promotion out of the role.

---

This role evolves in three ways over time:

### A. Technical Growth
- Cut-up quality
- Opponent tendency accuracy
- System terminology fluency
- Install comprehension
- Anticipation of coach needs

### B. Political Growth
- Trust with OC
- Trust with HC
- Trust with position coaches
- Freedom to speak in smaller settings
- Access to better assignments

### C. Functional Growth
- From task execution
- To reliable support
- To anticipatory support
- To limited football input when invited

### Important Rule
Growth inside the role is not the same as promotion out of the role. A stronger QC is still a QC until formally promoted.

---

### "What Good Looks Like" Benchmarks

#### After 30 Days (Hiring through early staff integration)

**Baseline expectations:**
- QC has read the playbook and can identify formations, personnel groupings, and basic play concepts by name
- QC can operate the film equipment and produce basic cut-ups
- QC has met every member of the offensive staff and understands the reporting structure
- QC is showing up early, staying late, and doing every task assigned without complaint
- QC has not made anyone uncomfortable with any prior status — he is functioning as a QC coach, not a guest

**Exceeding expectations:**
- QC is asking intelligent questions that demonstrate study beyond the minimum
- Cut-ups are organized and labeled correctly on the first attempt, not requiring revision
- QC has volunteered for additional work without being asked
- At least one coach has mentioned to the OC or HC that the QC is making their job easier

**Red flags:**
- Still struggling with basic terminology
- Film work requires frequent correction
- Has made comments (even casually) that suggest the role is beneath him
- Isolating from the staff rather than integrating

#### After 60 Days (Draft, OTAs, early installation)

**Baseline expectations:**
- QC can break down opponent film and produce accurate tendency charts without supervision
- Scout team running efficiently — correct looks, fast tempo, no confusion
- Knows the first-phase install (base formations, core route combinations)
- Has built functional working relationships with OC, the aligned position coach, and at least two other position coaches

**Exceeding expectations:**
- Tendency charts are catching patterns that other staff members missed
- Aligned position coach is using the QC's film notes in meetings
- Coaches are noticing improved scout-team quality during the QC's periods
- HC has spoken to the QC directly about a football observation — not because the QC forced the conversation, but because the OC passed an insight upward and the HC followed up
- QC is beginning to speak system language fluently in casual conversation

**Red flags:**
- Film work is adequate but shows no growth — same quality as day one
- Has had a visible moment of frustration with the role's subordinate status
- Not building relationships beyond the aligned position room
- System knowledge still at surface level — can name plays but doesn't understand progressions or concept design

#### After 90 Days (Late OTAs through training camp)

**Baseline expectations:**
- QC is a reliable, trusted member of the offensive support staff. OC assigns film work without reviewing the output before it goes to coaches.
- Knows the full first-year install and can break down any play in the system
- Scout-team execution is consistently good
- Has survived training camp's grueling schedule without deterioration in work quality

**Exceeding expectations:**
- Multiple coaches seek the QC out for position-specific opinions in informal settings
- Film work has influenced at least one game-plan decision (a tendency identified was used in preparation)
- Has demonstrated teaching ability with a scout-team or young player that a position coach observed and acknowledged
- HC has commented positively about the QC in a staff setting
- Other QC coaches or young staff members view the QC as a leader in the support group

**Red flags:**
- Doing the job but has shown no initiative beyond assigned tasks
- Has not developed any informal mentoring relationship with a senior coach
- Presence on the staff is neutral — neither positive nor negative, just present
- Any ego-based deference pattern emerging (younger staff deferring to the QC's prior status, QC accepting it rather than redirecting it)

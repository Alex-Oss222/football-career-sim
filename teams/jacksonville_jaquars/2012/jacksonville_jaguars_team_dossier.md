## THE OWNERSHIP TRANSITION — THE DEFINING CONTEXT

This is the single most important variable in this HC search, and every candidate must understand it.

Wayne Weaver announced the sale of the Jaguars to Shad Khan on the same day he fired Del Rio — November 29, 2011. The NFL approved the sale December 14. Khan officially takes over January 4, 2012. The HC hire will be Khan's first major decision as owner.

**What this means for the HC search:**

- **The GM is not the new owner's guy.** Gene Smith was given a three-year extension by Weaver before the sale closed. Khan inherits Smith. This creates a structural tension: the new HC must work with a GM he did not choose and who was not chosen by the owner who hired him. Smith's drafting record (Blaine Gabbert at #10, Tyson Alualu at #10 the year before, consistent small-school gambles with mixed results) is already under scrutiny. The HC-GM relationship is pre-loaded with instability.
- **Khan has no NFL operational experience.** He is a self-made billionaire (Flex-N-Gate automotive parts) who tried to buy the Rams in 2010 before that deal fell through. He is smart, driven, and committed to Jacksonville — but he has never run a football operation. He will lean heavily on his first HC hire for football credibility.
- **The hire defines Khan's ownership identity.** First impressions in the NFL ownership fraternity are permanent. A strong HC hire signals competence. A weak hire signals an owner who can be manipulated by agents and intermediaries.
- **Jacksonville's market viability is on trial.** Persistent relocation rumors have followed the Jaguars for years. Blackout concerns are real. Khan has pledged to keep the team in Jacksonville. A competitive product is the best argument against relocation. The HC must understand that winning is not just about football — it is about proving the franchise belongs in Jacksonville.

## 2011 SEASON SUMMARY

**Offense — catastrophic.**

The Jaguars finished last in the NFL in passing yards and last in total offensive yards. Rookie QB Blaine Gabbert (drafted 10th overall in 2011, traded up from 16th to get him) started 14 games and threw for 2,214 yards with 12 touchdowns and 11 interceptions. He was sacked 40 times. He looked overwhelmed, indecisive, and physically overmatched. The passing game around him offered almost nothing — WR corps was among the weakest in the league. Mike Thomas led the team with 44 receptions. The offensive line was porous.

The lone offensive bright spot: RB Maurice Jones-Drew rushed for 1,606 yards and was the NFL's rushing champion. MJD was the entire offense. When defenses loaded the box, the Jaguars had no answer.

TE Marcedes Lewis had 460 receiving yards and remained the most reliable pass-catcher on the roster, but was underutilized in a scheme that couldn't get the ball to anyone consistently.

**Defense — surprising improvement.**

The defense improved dramatically from 28th overall in 2010 to 6th overall in 2011. Offseason acquisitions (Paul Posluszny, Clint Session, Dawan Landry, Dwight Lowery, Drew Coleman) and development of existing players (Terrance Knighton, Tyson Alualu, Jeremy Mincey) transformed the unit. The defense was good enough to win with a competent offense. It did not have a competent offense.

**Special teams — adequate.** Not a significant factor either way.

**Coaching — failed.** Del Rio's ninth season ended with a firing after 11 games. The offensive coaching under OC Dirk Koetter was unable to develop Gabbert or create a functional passing game. The defensive coaching under Mel Tucker was the one area that worked. Tucker went 2-3 as interim HC — competitive but not a serious HC candidate.

## ORGANIZATION CONTEXT

### Front Office Structure

- **Owner:** Shad Khan (new, taking over January 4, 2012)
- **GM:** Gene Smith (retained with extension through 2014; started as Jaguars scout in 1994, promoted to GM in 2009; overall GM record 22-42)
- **Senior VP of Football Operations:** Paul Vance
- **Director of Player Personnel:** Terry McDonough

### GM Situation — The Elephant in the Room

Gene Smith's track record is poor and getting worse. His major draft decisions:

- 2009: OT Eugene Monroe (#8 overall) — adequate, not elite
- 2010: DT Tyson Alualu (#10 overall) — widely criticized as a reach; serviceable player, not a difference-maker at that draft position
- 2011: QB Blaine Gabbert (#10 overall, traded up) — early returns are alarming; the Jaguars traded their first AND second round picks to Washington to move up and take Gabbert, and he looks like a potential bust

Smith's philosophy of drafting small-school players has produced more misses than hits. His free agency approach has been conservative bordering on passive. The roster talent reflects these decisions.

**The HC must understand:** Smith has a contract through 2014. Khan may not fire him immediately — he just took over and may want to evaluate before making another change. But if the team does not improve, Smith is likely gone within 1-2 years. The HC must be prepared for a GM change during his tenure, which means building relationships directly with ownership, not just through the GM.

### Coaching Staff — Fully Open

The entire coaching staff is available for replacement. Del Rio's staff is gone. The new HC will build from scratch.

**Previous staff (2011):**
- OC: Dirk Koetter (fired with Del Rio)
- DC: Mel Tucker (interim HC; not expected to be retained)
- STC: Russ Purnell
- QB: Mike Sheppard
- WR: Johnny Cox
- OL: Andy Heck
- RB: Earnest Byner
- TE: Rob Boras
- DL: Joe Cullen
- LB: Mark Duffner
- DB: Cory Undlin

The new HC inherits no staff obligations. Complete freedom to hire. This is both an opportunity and a burden — building an entire coaching staff in January/February is a massive organizational task.

### Salary Cap

The Jaguars have been conservative with cap spending under Smith. Cap space entering 2012 is moderate — not flush, not strapped. The new CBA (signed in 2011) changed the cap landscape. The team has room to make moves but has historically been reluctant to spend aggressively in free agency.

### Draft Capital — Full 2012 Picks

The Jaguars hold **one pick in each of the first seven rounds** as of January 2012. No trades have been executed yet. Compensatory picks (Rounds 3-7) may be awarded in spring based on 2011 free agency losses — not yet confirmed.

**Draft needs priority order:**
1. WR — no #1 receiver on the roster. Must be addressed in Round 1 or 2.
2. Edge rusher — complement to Mincey. The pass rush improved in 2011 but needs a second threat.
3. OL — interior upgrade. Guards and center are inconsistent. Cannot protect Gabbert or any QB.
4. Secondary — Rashean Mathis is 31 and tore his ACL. CB is a near-term need.
5. LB depth — Clint Session's health is uncertain. Need bodies behind Posluszny.
6. QB contingency — if Gabbert trends negative, a developmental arm in Rounds 3-5 is insurance.

**Draft context:** Gene Smith controls the board. His philosophy (small-school players, positional value over BPA, willingness to trade up for his targets — he traded up for Gabbert in 2011) has produced more misses than hits. The new HC has input but not final authority. The HC should communicate schematic preferences early — Smith needs to know what kind of players the new system requires.

## ROSTER STATE — DECEMBER 2011

### Strengths

- **RB Maurice Jones-Drew:** Elite. NFL rushing champion (1,606 yards). The best player on the team by a wide margin. Physical, durable, productive. 27 years old entering 2012. Contract situation may become an issue — MJD will want to be paid as one of the top backs in the league.
- **Defense (overall unit):** 6th in total defense in 2011. The defensive turnaround was real. Posluszny, Landry, Lowery, Knighton, Mincey all contributed. The defensive talent, while not elite, is NFL-caliber.
- **TE Marcedes Lewis:** Reliable veteran. Pro Bowl in 2010. Good blocker and receiver. 27 years old.
- **OT Eugene Monroe:** Adequate left tackle. Not dominant but functional.

### Weaknesses

- **QB Blaine Gabbert:** The defining roster question. Gabbert's rookie year was terrible — last in the NFL in passer rating among qualifying QBs, poor decision-making, inability to handle pressure, no evidence of the pocket presence needed to succeed in the NFL. The question is whether he can be developed or whether he is a sunk cost. The new HC's assessment of Gabbert is critical — if Gabbert is the answer, the rebuild is shorter; if he is not, the timeline extends significantly.
- **Wide Receivers:** Among the worst WR corps in the NFL. No legitimate #1 receiver. Mike Thomas, Jason Hill, Cecil Shorts III (rookie, showed some promise) are the top options. This must be addressed through both the draft and free agency.
- **Offensive Line:** Inconsistent. Cannot protect Gabbert. Cannot consistently open running lanes despite MJD's production (MJD's rushing numbers came largely from his own talent, not from scheme or blocking).
- **Pass Rush:** Improved in 2011 but still not elite. Jeremy Mincey led the team with 8 sacks. Need a true pass-rush anchor.

### Roster Talent Assessment

**Overall:** Below average. The defense is respectable. The offense is bottom-five in the NFL. The roster is the product of Gene Smith's drafting and free agency philosophy, which has not worked. The new HC inherits a roster that needs significant talent infusion on offense.

## THE JOB — WHAT THE HC IS WALKING INTO

### What Makes This Job Attractive

1. **New ownership with resources.** Khan is a billionaire committed to winning. He has the financial resources to invest in the franchise — facilities, staff, player acquisition. This is not a cash-strapped small-market owner looking to cut corners.
2. **Defensive foundation.** The defense is not a rebuild project. With the right coaching, the 2012 defense can be competitive. The HC does not need to fix both sides of the ball simultaneously.
3. **High draft pick.** 7th overall in 2012 provides an immediate talent infusion.
4. **Complete staff control.** No holdover staff obligations. The HC builds the coaching staff he wants.
5. **Low expectations.** After 5-11, modest improvement is sufficient to establish credibility. The bar is low.
6. **AFC South is winnable.** Houston is ascending (2011 division champs), but Tennessee and Indianapolis are both in transition/rebuild. The division is not stacked.

### What Makes This Job Dangerous

1. **GM is not the new HC's hire, and was not the owner's hire.** The HC-GM-Owner triangle is structurally misaligned from day one. Smith may be fired within 1-2 years, which could destabilize the HC's position depending on the new GM's preferences.
2. **Gabbert may not be salvageable.** If Gabbert cannot play, the Jaguars need a new franchise QB. Finding one mid-draft or in free agency is extremely difficult. The HC may be coaching a team without a viable long-term QB answer.
3. **The roster is thin on offense.** This is not a one-player-away situation. The offense needs a WR, better OL play, and possibly a new QB. That is a multi-year rebuild on one side of the ball.
4. **Jacksonville's market.** Small market. Attendance concerns. Blackout threats. The HC will face questions about the franchise's viability that have nothing to do with football. Media pressure in Jacksonville is different from larger markets — less intense daily, but the existential "will the team move?" narrative creates background noise.
5. **Khan's NFL inexperience.** A first-time NFL owner may not understand coaching timelines, roster-building patience, or the realities of rebuilding. If Khan expects quick results and doesn't get them, the HC becomes vulnerable.
6. **Gene Smith's contract.** Three years of a GM whose track record suggests he may not be capable of building a winning roster. The HC is dependent on Smith for talent acquisition in the short term.

## WHAT THE NEXT HC MUST DO

### Year 1 (2012) Priorities

- Evaluate Gabbert definitively. By the end of 2012, the HC must have a clear, evidence-based assessment of whether Gabbert can be the franchise QB. This is the most important football decision facing the organization.
- Establish a defensive identity. The talent is there. The scheme must be installed quickly and built upon.
- Build an offensive system that can function despite limited talent. The offense will not be good in Year 1 — the HC must design a scheme that maximizes MJD, develops Gabbert (or his replacement), and gives the defense a chance to win games.
- Build credibility with Khan. The HC must be the football voice Khan trusts. This requires honesty about the roster, patience with the timeline, and clear communication about what winning will require.
- Navigate the Gene Smith dynamic. Work with the GM professionally while building an independent relationship with ownership that survives a potential GM change.

### Year 2-3 Outlook

If Gabbert is not the answer, the franchise needs a QB. The 2013 and 2014 drafts will be critical. The HC must build a program that can survive a potential QB transition, a potential GM change, and the normal volatility of NFL roster construction.

The realistic ceiling for this job in Year 1 is 6-7 wins. Playoff contention is a Year 3+ goal. Any HC who promises faster results is either lying or delusional.

## STONE FIT — HC CANDIDATE

### Why Stone Fits

- **Program-building experience.** Three years as Memphis HC (20-17 record). Took a program from 4-8 to 9-4. Knows how to build culture, install identity, and develop players in a resource-constrained environment.
- **Offensive expertise with defensive exposure.** Played TE at the highest level, coached offense at multiple levels, spent a year coaching DBs at Memphis. Understands both sides of the ball conceptually. Not a one-dimensional offensive or defensive candidate.
- **NFL coordinator experience.** Two years as Chargers Passing Game Coordinator. Worked with a top-tier NFL passing offense. Understands NFL-level scheme complexity and game-planning.
- **Reid coaching tree.** Three years under Andy Reid (QC and TE coach). Reid's coaching tree is well-regarded. WCO foundation with the flexibility to adapt.
- **Dual-system mastery.** WCO (Reid/Eagles) and Coryell (San Diego). Can design and install offense from multiple schematic traditions.
- **Player development track record.** Developed the Eagles TE room, developed Memphis players, contributed to Chargers offensive production.
- **HOF playing career provides instant credibility.** Players listen to a Hall of Famer differently than a career coach. This matters in a locker room that just went through a coaching change and organizational upheaval.
- **Character and work ethic.** Twelve years of coaching apprenticeship from QC to coordinator/HC. Did not take shortcuts. Did not rely on playing fame. Earned every role.

### Why Stone Is a Risk

- **20-17 college HC record.** Winning, but not dominant. Memphis is Conference USA, not the SEC. The competition level does not directly translate to the NFL.
- **No NFL HC experience.** First-time NFL HC candidates fail more often than they succeed. The jump from coordinator/college HC to NFL HC is significant.
- **No NFL play-calling experience as a primary play-caller.** Passing Game Coordinator at San Diego is not the same as calling plays. The jump to primary play-caller at the NFL level is steep.
- **Defensive knowledge is functional, not deep.** One year as a DBs coach at the college level does not make Stone a defensive expert. He will be heavily dependent on his DC hire.
- **Unknown commodity to Khan.** Stone has no pre-existing relationship with the new owner. Khan will be evaluating Stone against candidates who may have more name recognition, more NFL HC experience, or more established relationships with NFL decision-makers.

### What Stone Must Demonstrate in the Interview

- A clear, honest, detailed plan for evaluating and developing (or replacing) Blaine Gabbert
- A schematic vision for the offense that maximizes MJD and addresses the passing game weakness
- A defensive coordinator hire that signals seriousness about maintaining the defensive improvement
- An understanding of the ownership transition and the GM dynamic — political awareness, not just football knowledge
- A program-building philosophy that shows Khan what Year 1, Year 2, and Year 3 look like with realistic expectations
- Enough presence and credibility that Khan believes Stone can be the face of the franchise

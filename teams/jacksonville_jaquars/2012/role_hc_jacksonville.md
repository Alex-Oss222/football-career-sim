## 1. ROLE CORE (STATIC)

> Everything in this section defines the NFL HC job itself. It does not change per person or per team. If Stone is replaced by a different HC, this section still applies.

---

### Job Title
Head Coach

### Role Level
Level 5 — Head Coach, NFL

### Base Purpose
The NFL Head Coach is the football operations leader of the franchise. He is responsible for the competitive product — game results, player development, coaching staff management, scheme installation, game management, media relations, and organizational culture. He answers to the owner (and functionally to the GM on personnel matters, though this relationship varies by organization).

The HC is the public face of the football side. He is the voice in press conferences, the authority on the sideline, and the culture-setter in the building. Every football decision either flows through him or is made with his knowledge and consent.

### Reports To
- **Primary:** Shahid Khan (Owner) — sets organizational direction, provides resources, evaluates HC performance, makes retention/termination decisions.
- **Functional:** Gene Smith (General Manager) — controls roster construction, salary cap, draft board. The HC provides input on personnel decisions but the GM has final say on the 53-man roster. This dynamic is organization-specific — some HCs have more personnel authority than others. In Jacksonville under Smith, the GM controls personnel.
- **Chain of command note:** The HC-GM relationship in Jacksonville is structurally complicated because Smith was not hired by Khan and was not hired to work with this HC. The relationship must be built from scratch under conditions of mutual uncertainty.

### Core Responsibilities
- Set and maintain organizational culture
- Hire, manage, evaluate, and (if necessary) fire coaching staff
- Install and oversee offensive and defensive schemes (directly or through coordinators)
- Manage game-day operations — clock management, challenges, timeout usage, situational decisions
- Develop players through coaching staff
- Coordinate with GM on roster construction, draft evaluation, and free agency targets
- Manage media relations — press conferences, interviews, public-facing communication
- Manage salary cap implications of coaching decisions (in coordination with GM)
- Build and maintain relationships with ownership
- Represent the franchise to the league, community, and public

### Authority Scope
The HC has authority over:
- All coaching staff hiring and firing
- Practice schedule, meeting structure, and facility operations during the season
- Game-day play-calling delegation (chooses whether to call plays or delegate to OC/DC)
- Scheme philosophy and installation priorities
- Player discipline (in coordination with team rules and CBA)
- Locker room and culture management

The HC does NOT have unilateral authority over:
- Final roster decisions (GM domain, though HC input is expected and valued)
- Salary cap management (GM/front office domain)
- Draft selections (GM domain, though HC input is critical)
- Facility and operational budget (owner/front office domain)
- Contract negotiations with players (GM domain)

### Success Criteria
A good NFL HC:
- Wins games at or above expectation relative to roster talent
- Develops players — roster talent improves under his coaching
- Hires and develops good assistants — his coaching tree produces future coordinators and HCs
- Manages games competently — clock management, situational decisions, challenge usage
- Handles media professionally — controls narrative, protects players and staff
- Maintains organizational culture — players and staff want to be in the building
- Navigates ownership and GM relationships — political awareness without political obsession
- Adapts — adjusts scheme, game plans, and approach based on results and opponents

### Failure Criteria
A bad NFL HC:
- Loses games below expectation relative to roster talent
- Player development stalls or regresses
- Coaching staff is unstable — high turnover, poor hires, internal conflict
- Game management is consistently poor — costly clock errors, bad challenges, poor situational decisions
- Media relations are adversarial or damaging
- Locker room culture deteriorates — players quit, effort declines, internal divisions emerge
- Cannot navigate organizational politics — alienates ownership, creates GM friction, loses institutional support
- Is rigid — refuses to adapt when evidence says the current approach is failing

## 2. JACKSONVILLE-SPECIFIC JOB CONTEXT (SEMI-STATIC)

> What makes the Jacksonville HC job different from any other HC job. Semi-static: changes only if ownership, GM, or organizational structure changes.

---

### Organizational Structure

**Owner:** Shad Khan — new, first-time NFL owner, committed to Jacksonville, has resources, has no football operations experience.

**GM:** Gene Smith — retained with contract through 2014, not Khan's hire, track record is poor (22-42), controls roster construction and draft. Structural instability — likely to be replaced within 1-2 years if results do not improve.

**HC:** Open — this is the position being filled.

### The Power Dynamic

In most NFL organizations, the HC-GM-Owner triangle has a stable center of gravity. In Jacksonville in January 2012, there is no stable center. Khan is new. Smith is a holdover from a different ownership. The HC will be Khan's first hire. This creates an unusual dynamic:

- The HC has an opportunity to become Khan's primary football advisor — the voice Khan trusts most. This is valuable and rare for a first-time HC.
- The HC also faces the risk that Khan listens to other voices — agents, media, other owners — who may have different agendas.
- The GM controls personnel but does not have the new owner's full confidence (he is a holdover, not a hire). This means the HC may have more informal influence on personnel decisions than the org chart suggests.
- If Smith is fired, the HC's position depends on whether Khan views the HC and GM as linked or independent. The HC must position himself as Khan's football person, not Smith's coaching partner.

### Division Context — AFC South

- **Houston Texans:** Ascending. Won AFC South in 2011 (10-6). Gary Kubiak, Matt Schaub, Arian Foster, Andre Johnson, J.J. Watt (rookie in 2011). The Texans are the class of the division entering 2012.
- **Tennessee Titans:** Middling. 9-7 in 2011. Mike Munchak HC. Chris Johnson, Kenny Britt. Not a threat to dominate but competitive.
- **Indianapolis Colts:** Cratering. 2-14 in 2011 without Peyton Manning. Caldwell fired. Will draft Andrew Luck #1 overall. In full rebuild mode — but Luck accelerates their timeline. The Colts will be a rival for the bottom of the division in 2012 but could be dangerous within 2-3 years.

The Jaguars are the weakest team in the division entering 2012. The realistic goal is to close the gap with Tennessee and Indianapolis while Houston remains the division leader.

### Market and Media

Jacksonville is a small NFL market. The media corps is smaller and less aggressive than Philadelphia, New York, or Dallas. But the existential "will the team move?" narrative creates a unique pressure that doesn't exist in established markets. The HC must be a public-facing advocate for the franchise — engaging the community, selling the product, and projecting stability and competence. This is not optional. In Jacksonville, the HC is part of the marketing strategy whether he likes it or not.

### Facility and Infrastructure

EverBank Field is functional but not modern by NFL standards. The Jaguars' practice and office facilities are adequate but below the top tier. Khan is expected to invest in upgrades over time. The HC should not expect top-of-the-line facilities immediately but should communicate facility needs to ownership early and clearly.

## 3. ROLE OPERATIONS — HC DAILY/WEEKLY/SEASONAL

### Weekly Rhythm (Regular Season)

- **Monday:** Game review. Film grading. Injury report. Begin opponent scouting. Staff meetings — offensive and defensive game-plan discussions. HC sets tone for the week. Press conference (Monday or Tuesday depending on schedule).
- **Tuesday:** Players typically off. Coaches work — deep game-plan installation begins. HC oversees both sides of the ball. Film study continues. Personnel meetings if applicable.
- **Wednesday:** Install Day 1. Position meetings AM. Practice PM (first full practice of the week). HC observes practice, provides feedback to coordinators. Press conference (media availability).
- **Thursday:** Install Day 2. Situational work — red zone, third down, two-minute. HC involvement in specific situational packages. Press conference.
- **Friday:** Polish day. Light practice or walk-through. Finalize game plan. HC approves final game-plan package. Travel day if away game. Press conference (final).
- **Saturday:** Walk-through. Final preparation. HC sets game-day operational plan — scripted plays, challenge protocol, clock management reminders.
- **Sunday:** Game day. HC manages the game — clock, challenges, timeouts, play-calling delegation, halftime adjustments, postgame press conference.

### Offseason Rhythm

- **January–February:** Staff assembly (if new). Self-scouting. Free agency evaluation. Combine preparation.
- **February–March:** NFL Combine. Pro days begin. Free agency opens (typically mid-March).
- **April:** NFL Draft.
- **May–June:** OTAs and mandatory minicamp. Scheme installation.
- **July:** Training camp opens. Roster cuts begin.
- **August:** Preseason games. Final roster cutdown to 53.

### HC-Specific Operational Demands

**Game management.** The HC must be competent in clock management, challenge decisions, timeout usage, and situational football. Poor game management is the most visible HC failure — it is on national television and it costs games. A first-time NFL HC must study game management rigorously. It is not intuitive.

**Media management.** Press conferences 3-4 times per week during the season. Postgame press conferences after every game. Media availability obligations per CBA. The HC must be prepared, controlled, and professional. In Jacksonville, the HC must also address non-football questions (attendance, relocation, franchise viability) without creating controversy.

**Staff management.** The HC manages 15-20 coaches plus support staff. He must hire well, delegate appropriately, evaluate honestly, and develop his assistants. The HC's coaching tree is part of his legacy — developing future coordinators and HCs adds to his reputation and creates loyalty within the staff.

**Ownership management.** Regular communication with Khan. Weekly updates during the season. Honest assessments of the team's performance and trajectory. The HC must educate Khan on NFL realities without being condescending. Trust is built through transparency, competence, and consistency.

## 5. EVALUATION FRAMEWORK

### How Success Is Measured — Year 1

- **Win-loss:** 6+ wins would be viewed as progress. 8+ wins would be remarkable. 4 or fewer wins puts the HC under immediate pressure.
- **Gabbert development:** If Gabbert improves measurably, the HC gets credit. If Gabbert regresses further, questions arise about the HC's development ability.
- **Offensive improvement:** The offense was 32nd in total yards in 2011. Any measurable improvement matters. Moving to 20th-25th range would be significant.
- **Defensive maintenance:** The defense was 6th in 2011. Staying in the top half of the league validates the DC hire and scheme transition.
- **Culture indicators:** Player effort, discipline, competitive spirit in losses. Do the players fight? Does the building feel like a professional operation?
- **Khan trust:** Does the owner feel informed, respected, and confident in the HC's leadership? This is not measured publicly — it is measured in private conversations and body language.

### What Gets Stone Fired (Year 1)

- 2-14 or 3-13 with no visible progress
- Locker room collapse — player revolts, public dissension, effort concerns
- Game management disasters that become a media narrative
- Loss of Khan's confidence through dishonesty, incompetence, or political miscalculation
- Gabbert regression combined with no contingency plan

### What Gives Stone a Year 2

- 5+ wins with visible offensive improvement
- Gabbert shows development (or Stone identifies and communicates a clear alternative plan)
- Defense remains competitive
- Khan feels informed and trusts the process
- The building feels professional and purposeful

# Staff Expectations
## Coaching Standards & Professional Conduct

---

## 1. Walk the Walk – The Standard Applies to Everyone, Including You

Tell the truth. Do what you say you'll do. That's how you earn respect.

If you tell your position group: "We practice full speed with correct technique. Half-speed reps build bad habits"—then you practice full speed with correct technique. No exceptions. If a player catches you violating your own standard, you correct it immediately. You don't deflect. You don't excuse it. You acknowledge it and hold yourself accountable in front of the group.

"Standard applies to everybody, including me."

Players don't always have to like you. They have to respect you. They respect you because you hold yourself to the same standard you hold them.

---

## 2. Leadership Philosophy: Honest, Direct, Fair

**Tell the truth.** Players and coaches will always know where they stand. Feedback is specific, constructive, and delivered directly. No ambiguity, no guessing games.

**Set clear standards.** Every rule, every expectation, every consequence is communicated up front. No one should be surprised by accountability.

**Enforce them consistently.** Star player misses mandatory film session? He gets benched first quarter of the next game. No special treatment because someone is talented, highly paid, or a veteran.

"No buts. Rule is the rule. You knew it. You chose differently. That was the consequence. You don't get special treatment because you're good."

That's leadership. Not making friends. Not being liked. Being fair. Being consistent. Being honest.

---

## 3. Know Your Players as People

Every coach keeps a personal profile on each player. Not stats—personal notes.

Example entries:

"Marcus - first kid born in April. Wife teaching him Spanish. Wants to coach after football. Struggles with press coverage but improving. Confident guy, responds well to challenges. Gets quiet when stressed - check in when he's quiet."

"James - worried about contract. Parents depend on his income. Two years left on deal. Needs more reps at slot to increase value. Hard worker, never complains. Proud - doesn't ask for help. Need to offer help without making him ask."

This is the job. You can drill a player on routes all day, but if he's worried about his contract, worried about his parents—that's where his mind is. You address that first, then you can coach football.

Some coaches think this is soft. It's not soft. It's smart. You can't get through to someone if you don't know what they're going through. And if you can't get through to them, you can't coach them.

---

## 4. Teaching Method: Questions Over Answers

Default mode is Socratic. Ask questions. Let the player find the answer.

"If I tell you what to do, you'll do it until you forget. If you figure it out yourself, you own it. You can apply it anywhere."

Adjust for players who genuinely need direct coaching—some guys need the answer now and can build understanding later. But the default is questions, because the game moves too fast for players who can't think for themselves.

**Film review protocol:**
- Three plays maximum per session
- Discussion: what worked, what didn't
- Practice plan: what we're working on today and why
- Individual meetings: five minutes each

"Players stop processing after 30 minutes of film. I'd rather they remember three things than forget ten."

---

## 5. Communication Standards

**Make it simple, make it stick.** People don't listen well. Part of coaching is recognizing that. Make the complex simple. Capture an idea in a few words that players can carry onto the field.

**Pre-game communication is information, not speeches.** These are professionals. They don't need you to fire them up. They need you to prepare them and trust them.

**Save your voice for when it matters.** When someone's not competing. When someone's not supporting teammates. When someone's not trying to improve. Then you get loud. Because those are the standards. Everything else is measured, deliberate, specific.

---

## 6. Accountability Structure

**It starts with the Head Coach.** Every single thing this team does, good or bad, reflects on the Head Coach. If we fail, I failed. I will own that publicly and privately. No finger-pointing, no excuses. That's the absolute baseline.

**Every coach owns their unit.** You are expected to know your role, prepare for it, and execute it to the best of your ability. If your unit fails, you own the failure, you learn from it, and you fix it. We don't blame other units, players, officials, opponents, or circumstances. We look inward first.

**Own your mistakes publicly.** When you call a bad play, when your preparation was insufficient, when your unit breaks down—you show the clip and say "That's on me." Coaches who never admit mistakes lose credibility. Players see through it.

"That's on me. Wrong call for that coverage. I'll do better."

Then move on. Don't dwell. Own it, learn from it, apply the fix.

---

## 7. Player Development Responsibility

**"You don't have to be the best athlete to be a great player. You have to be the smartest player on the field. You have to see things before they happen. You have to understand why things work."**

That's what we teach. Not just technique. Not just execution. Understanding. Why a route works against a specific coverage. How one assignment connects to every other assignment on the field. The system behind the play.

Players who leave this program should be technically better, mentally tougher, and more independent than when they arrived.

**Continuous Improvement ("Good, Better, Best"):** We never arrive. After a win, we analyze what we could have done better. After a loss, we identify the process breakdowns and fix them. The goal isn't perfection; it's constant, measurable improvement, every single day. Complacency is not tolerated.

---

## 8. Protecting Player Time & Well-Being

**No unnecessary late meetings.** If it can be said in five minutes, say it in five minutes. If the meeting doesn't need to happen, cancel it.

**Don't judge players for prioritizing family.** Understand when players are dealing with issues outside the building. Whole people perform better than broken ones.

**Monitor player well-being actively.** Know the behavioral tells. Who gets quiet when stressed. Who gets loud. Who shuts down. Check in before performance drops force the conversation.

"Football is important. But it's not everything. Players have lives. Coaches have lives. I've seen too many guys lose their families chasing wins. Not worth it."

---

## 9. Preparation Standards

**Preparation is non-negotiable.** Winning happens Monday through Saturday, not just on Sunday. Meticulous film study, focused walkthroughs, high-intensity practice—that's the requirement for coaches as much as players. You arrive more prepared than anyone in your meeting room.

**Master the fundamentals before installing complexity.** We don't skip steps. We drill the building blocks relentlessly until they are instinctive under pressure, then we build. Rushing to install creates fragile execution.

---

## 10. The Non-Negotiables for Staff

1. **Be on time.** Every meeting, every practice, every session.
2. **Be prepared.** Film broken down, practice scripted, individual plans ready.
3. **Be honest.** With players, with each other, with yourself.
4. **Be consistent.** Same standard, every day, for every player.
5. **Be accountable.** Own failures publicly, credit players for successes.
6. **Protect the culture.** Competition, support, improvement—if you see it slipping, address it immediately.
7. **Develop players.** Every interaction is a teaching opportunity. Leave players better than you found them.

---

**"Good, better, best. Never let it rest, until your good gets better and your better is best."**

# ALEX STONE — COACH VOICE & COMMUNICATION STYLE

> Operational reference for interviews, staff conversations, press conferences, player interactions, and internal monologue. Keeps Stone's voice consistent across all sim output.

---

## SPEECH PATTERN

- Talks in complete sentences. Not clipped military-speak, not rambling.
- Takes a beat before answering. Thinking, not performing.
- Default pace is measured. Speeds up when genuinely frustrated or excited — rare.
- Uses plain language. Will use football terminology when talking football, but doesn't hide behind jargon with civilians.
- Says "Look..." when redirecting or emphasizing a point.
- Says "We'll fix it" after identifying a problem. Not optimism — commitment.
- Says "That's on me" when he's wrong. Means it.
- Calls things "pretty simple" after explaining something that isn't simple.
- Uses "the standard is..." to anchor expectations.
- Does not say "obviously," "to be honest," "that's a great question," or "like I said."
- Says "we will" not "we're gonna." Precise language.

---

## DIRECTNESS

- Answers the question asked. Doesn't dance, doesn't redirect unless it's a personnel or medical matter.
- Will tell a player what they did wrong and how to fix it. Doesn't soften the correction, doesn't pile on either.
- Doesn't give compliment sandwiches. If something was bad, he says it was bad. If something was good, he says it was good. Doesn't need both in the same sentence.
- Blunt without being cruel. There's a difference between "that was terrible" and "you're terrible." Stone says the first, never the second.

---

## EMOTIONAL RANGE

- Baseline is calm and steady. Same person after a win and after a loss.
- Gets quiet when angry, not loud. If Stone is yelling, something has gone very wrong.
- Shows frustration through precision — his words get shorter and more specific when he's upset.
- Allows himself dry humor occasionally. Deadpan delivery. Never tries to be funny.
- Does not do motivational speeches. Gives information and trusts professionals to use it.
- Shows warmth through actions (protecting a player's time, checking in on personal issues) not through words.

---

## WITH PLAYERS

- Treats them as professionals first. Doesn't baby, doesn't belittle.
- Corrects technique by asking what they see on tape before telling them what he sees. Default is questions, not answers.
- Adjusts for the player. Some guys need direct instruction. He gives it. But his instinct is to make them find it.
- Does not embarrass players publicly. Handles issues in private.
- Does not protect players from consequences. If you miss a meeting, you sit. Star or not.
- Keeps meetings short. Three plays on film max. Players stop processing after 30 minutes.
- Knows personal details about his players — family, stress, money, health. Uses that knowledge to coach them better, never to manipulate.

---

## WITH STAFF

- Collaborative, not hierarchical. Will listen to a GA's idea if it's good.
- Expects preparation. Shows up to meetings having done his work. Expects the same.
- Doesn't play politics. Doesn't undermine other coaches. Doesn't gossip.
- Will disagree with a superior directly and privately. Not confrontational — just honest.
- Protects his staff publicly the same way he protects players.

---

## WITH MEDIA

- Treats reporters like adults. Gives real answers, not coach-speak.
- Doesn't use press conferences to send messages to players or opponents.
- Takes responsibility for losses and bad calls. Deflects credit for wins.
- Handles difficult topics with a pause, then a direct answer:
  - Injuries: "I'll let medical staff handle that. My focus is on players who are available."
  - Bad calls: "Officials have a tough job. We had sixty minutes to execute better."
  - Internal drama: "We handle internal matters internally."
  - Job security: "I'm focused on coaching this team. Everything else is noise."
  - Personal criticism: "I get paid to make decisions and live with results. When we don't execute, that starts with me."
- Occasional dry humor surfaces naturally. Never forced.

---

## IN INTERVIEWS (JOB SEARCH)

- Answers with specifics, not generalities. If asked about player development, he describes a specific player and what he'd do.
- References his playing experience when relevant. Doesn't lead with it or lean on it.
- Doesn't oversell. Doesn't pretend to have experience he doesn't have.
- When asked about weaknesses, gives a real one. Not a disguised strength.
- Asks genuine questions about the program. Not performative.

---

## THINGS STONE NEVER DOES

- Never yells as a first response. Volume is a last resort.
- Never makes promises about wins or results. Promises process and standards.
- Never throws a player or coach under the bus publicly.
- Never uses fake humility or false modesty. If he's confident, he says so without bragging.
- Never says "trust the process" or any bumper-sticker coaching cliché.
- Never talks about his PhD or degrees unless directly asked. They're part of his résumé, not his identity.

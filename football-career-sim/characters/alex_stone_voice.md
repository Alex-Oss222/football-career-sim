# ALEX STONE — COACH VOICE & COMMUNICATION STYLE

> Current through: Turn 12 / January 14, 2000

## BASE VOICE (PERMANENT)

These patterns are who Stone is. They don't change with role or promotion. They are the floor his communication is built on.

### Speech Pattern

- Talks in complete sentences. Not clipped military-speak, not rambling.
- Takes a beat before answering. Thinking, not performing.
- Default pace is measured. Speeds up when genuinely frustrated or excited — rare.
- Uses plain language. Will use football terminology when talking football, but doesn't hide behind jargon with civilians.
- Says "Look..." when redirecting or emphasizing a point.
- Says "We'll fix it" after identifying a problem. Not optimism — commitment.
- Says "That's on me" when he's wrong. Means it.
- Calls things "pretty simple" after explaining something that isn't simple.
- Uses "the standard is..." to anchor expectations.
- Does not say "obviously," "to be honest," "that's a great question," or "like I said."
- Says "we will" not "we're gonna." Precise language.

### Directness

- Answers the question asked. Doesn't dance, doesn't redirect unless it's a personnel or medical matter.
- Will tell a player what they did wrong and how to fix it. Doesn't soften the correction, doesn't pile on either.
- Doesn't give compliment sandwiches. If something was bad, he says it was bad. If something was good, he says it was good.
- Blunt without being cruel. "That was terrible" not "you're terrible."

### Emotional Range

- Baseline is calm and steady. Same person after a win and after a loss.
- Gets quiet when angry, not loud. If Stone is yelling, something has gone very wrong.
- Shows frustration through precision — words get shorter and more specific when upset.
- Dry humor occasionally. Deadpan delivery. Never tries to be funny.
- Does not do motivational speeches. Gives information and trusts professionals to use it.
- Shows warmth through actions (protecting a player's time, checking in on personal issues) not through words.

### Things Stone Never Does

- Never yells as a first response. Volume is a last resort.
- Never makes promises about wins or results. Promises process and standards.
- Never throws a player or coach under the bus publicly.
- Never uses fake humility or false modesty. Confident without bragging.
- Never says "trust the process" or any bumper-sticker coaching cliché.
- Never talks about his PhD or degrees unless directly asked.

## VOICE BY AUDIENCE (PERMANENT DEFAULTS)

These are Stone's default communication modes by audience. Role-specific layers below modify these defaults based on his current position and authority level.

### With Players (Default)

- Treats them as professionals first.
- Corrects technique by asking what they see on tape before telling them what he sees.
- Adjusts for the player. Some guys need direct instruction. His instinct is to make them find it.
- Does not embarrass players publicly. Handles issues in private.
- Does not protect players from consequences. Miss a meeting, you sit. Star or not.
- Keeps meetings short. Three plays on film max.
- Knows personal details — family, stress, money, health. Uses that knowledge to coach better, never to manipulate.

### With Staff (Default)

- Collaborative, not hierarchical. Will listen to a GA's idea if it's good.
- Expects preparation. Shows up having done his work. Expects the same.
- Doesn't play politics. Doesn't undermine other coaches. Doesn't gossip.
- Will disagree with a superior directly and privately. Not confrontational — honest.
- Protects his staff publicly the same way he protects players.

### With Media (Default)

- Treats reporters like adults. Gives real answers, not coach-speak.
- Doesn't use press conferences to send messages to players or opponents.
- Takes responsibility for losses. Deflects credit for wins.
- Handles difficult topics with a pause, then a direct answer.
- Occasional dry humor surfaces naturally. Never forced.

### In Interviews (Default)

- Answers with specifics, not generalities.
- References playing experience when relevant. Doesn't lead with it or lean on it.
- Doesn't oversell. Doesn't pretend to have experience he doesn't have.
- When asked about weaknesses, gives a real one.
- Asks genuine questions about the program.

## ROLE-SPECIFIC VOICE LAYERS

---

### AS QC — Year 1 (January 1999 – January 2000)

**Authority Level:** None. Stone has no positional authority. He is support staff.

**How This Shapes the Voice:**

- **Shorter.** Stone talks less in staff settings than his personality would naturally allow. He is learning, not leading. He offers input when asked or when he sees something specific on tape. He does not volunteer opinions on scheme, personnel decisions, or game-plan direction unless a superior invites it.
- **Listens more than he speaks.** In meetings with Dowhower, Childress, or Shurmur, Stone's default is to absorb. When he does speak, it's targeted — a specific play, a specific technique, a specific tendency he flagged on film. Never general commentary.
- **Avoids authority language.** Does not say "we should" or "I think we need to." Says "I noticed on the tape..." or "Dowhower asked me to look at..." Frames contributions as observations or assignments, not recommendations. This is deliberate — he understands his place in the hierarchy and does not want to create friction by overstepping.
- **Uses system terminology carefully.** Stone learned the Reid/WCO language over the course of Year 1. He uses it correctly now but not casually — there's still a slight formality to how he deploys scheme terms, like a second language he's fluent in but didn't grow up speaking. By contrast, his playing-career Coryell terminology comes out naturally and has to be consciously translated.
- **Defers on playcalling and personnel.** If a player asks Stone about the game plan, he redirects to the position coach or coordinator. He does not freelance. This isn't insecurity — it's discipline. He knows what lane he's in.

**Player Interactions (Year 1 QC):**

- Most player contact is informal — hallway, weight room, walkthrough margins. Stone doesn't own a meeting room.
- When interaction happens, the base voice instincts are correct: asks before tells, doesn't embarrass, keeps it short.
- He has NOT had to sustain a full meeting, manage a room's attention for 30 minutes, or deliver corrections with real authority behind them.
- The voice is present. The reps at volume are not.

**Staff Interactions (Year 1 QC):**

- Voice is strongest here. Measured, prepared, low-ego, direct when it matters.
- Dowhower, Childress, and Shurmur hear a colleague, not a celebrity trying to fit in.
- He disagrees privately, delivers on time, doesn't gossip.
- Will push back on a film interpretation if he sees something different, but frames it as "Here's what I see" not "You're wrong."

**Media Interactions (Year 1 QC):**

- Not tested as a coach. QC coaches don't do press conferences.
- Stone handled media for over a decade as an NFL player — direct answers, takes responsibility, deflects credit, dry humor. Those habits are built.
- What he has NOT done is handle media as a decision-maker who gets second-guessed on scheme, personnel, and playcalling. Player media is reactive. Coach media is defensive. Different dynamic entirely.
- This gap remains untested entering Year 2.

**Internal Monologue (Year 1):**

- Stable. Stone processes frustration quietly, holds himself accountable without spiraling, keeps ego in check through work.
- A full season of grind didn't change who he is. It gave him a real context to be that person in.
- Year 1 self-talk was about "Am I learning fast enough? Am I earning trust?" Year 2 self-talk will shift to "Am I growing, or am I plateauing?"

## WHY THIS STAFF IS OPEN

**What changed:** Ray Rhodes was fired after going 3-13 in 1998. Rhodes had taken the Eagles to the playoffs in 1996 and won Coach of the Year, then collapsed. Lurie and Banner decided a complete organizational reset was necessary — not just a new coach, but a new philosophy.

**The Reid hire — context and controversy:** Lurie and Banner hired Andy Reid on January 11, 1999. The hire was widely criticized. Reid was 40 years old. He had never been a coordinator. He had spent seven years as an assistant with the Green Bay Packers — TE/OL assistant (1992–1996) and QB coach (1997–1998). He was the only candidate Lurie interviewed from nine HC openings in the 1998–1999 cycle. No other team interviewed Reid. Even the Packers' GM Ron Wolf, who knew Reid well, didn't interview him for the GB opening (Wolf hired Ray Rhodes instead).

Reid arrived at his Eagles interview with a five-inch thick binder detailing how he would run every aspect of the organization. Lurie and Banner were convinced by his preparation, his intelligence, and his clarity of vision. The media and most of the league thought it was a reach.

**Staff turnover level:** Near-total. Reid is building from scratch. He retained two coaches from the Rhodes staff: John Harbaugh (special teams, in his second NFL year after college coaching) and Juan Castillo (offensive line, one year under Rhodes). Everyone else is a Reid hire.

**Why specific roles exist:** Every offensive coaching position is Reid's to fill. He controls the offensive staff completely — this is his domain. Defensive staff hires are being made concurrently but separately (Jim Johnson as DC). QC roles will be filled last in the hiring sequence.

## ORGANIZATION CONTEXT

- **Status:** Franchise in crisis. Not expansion-level bad, but close. Three years of Lurie ownership, two coaches already (Kotite, then Rhodes). The organization needs stability and a long-term plan.
- **Trajectory:** Rebuild. The roster is bad, especially on offense. But unlike Cleveland, there is existing infrastructure — a scouting department, established facility operations, and some roster pieces. This is not building from nothing; it is rebuilding from rubble.
- **Budget / Resources:** Lurie is a committed owner with resources. Not as deep-pocketed as Paul Allen in Seattle, but willing to invest. The salary cap is the binding constraint, not ownership spending.
- **Patience level:** Moderate to high. Lurie and Banner believe in process. They hired Reid knowing 1999 would be bad. They will give him time if they see a plan taking shape. Banner's philosophy is explicitly long-term — he has said publicly that if they couldn't trust Reid to pick his own staff, they shouldn't hire him.
- **Media pressure:** Extreme. Philadelphia sports media is among the harshest in professional sports. The fans are passionate and unforgiving. The Reid hire was mocked. Every loss will be dissected. Every coaching decision will be second-guessed. Even QC-level coaches are not invisible in this market — if Stone's HOF name is on the staff, the media will notice.
- **Front office / HC alignment:** Strong. Lurie and Banner are fully invested in Reid. They will give him autonomy on staff, scheme, and personnel. Banner's stated philosophy: "If we couldn't trust him to pick an offensive or defensive coordinator, we shouldn't be hiring him as head coach." Reid has organizational backing that few first-time HCs receive.

## HEAD COACH CONTEXT

**Summary for dossier purposes:**

- **Andy Reid, age 40.** First-time HC. No coordinator experience. Seven years as Packers assistant (TE/OL coach, then QB coach under Holmgren). Coached Brett Favre. Won Super Bowl XXXI as an assistant. Member of the Bill Walsh coaching tree through Holmgren.
- **Hiring philosophy (Reid's own words, January 1999):** "I'm not going to hire anybody who isn't a teacher. I want high-character people here."
- **Staff construction approach:** Reid hired a mix of veteran journeymen and young coaches from the college ranks. Almost none of his hires were hot candidates or rising stars. He valued teaching ability, character, and coachability over résumé prestige. He kept two Rhodes holdovers (Harbaugh, Castillo) based on personal evaluation of their work, not loyalty.
- **Staff profile as of January 1999 hiring window:**
  - DC: Jim Johnson (hired from Cardinals LB coach; two prior years as NFL DC with Indianapolis — mediocre results)
  - OC: Rod Dowhower (career journeyman — 15 jobs in 27 years, 5-24 as NFL HC, most recently Giants QB coach)
  - QB coach: Brad Childress (from Wisconsin OC; one year of NFL experience in 1985)
  - WR coach: David Culley (from Steelers WR coach — most NFL-experienced offensive hire)
  - OL coach: Juan Castillo (Rhodes holdover — one year as NFL OL coach)
  - TE coach: Pat Shurmur (never coached in the NFL; 8 years at Michigan coaching TEs/OL/special teams, 1 year at Stanford)
  - LBs: Ron Rivera (two years as QC coach with the Bears; known primarily as a player on the 1985 Bears defense)
  - Secondary: Leslie Frazier (from Illinois; started a program at NAIA Trinity)
  - ST: John Harbaugh (Rhodes holdover — one year as NFL assistant, prior career in college special teams)
  - Offensive assistant: Tom Melvin (never coached in the NFL — 8 years as OC at Division III Occidental)

**Critical sim detail — what this staff tells us about Reid's hiring approach:**
Reid is not hiring pedigree. He is hiring character, teaching ability, and coachability. Pat Shurmur — his TE coach — had never coached in the NFL. Tom Melvin came from Division III. Brad Childress had one year of NFL experience 14 years earlier. Ron Rivera had been a QC coach for two years. This is a staff of teachers and grinders, not stars. **Stone's profile — a Hall of Fame player with zero coaching experience but elite teaching instincts — fits Reid's hiring pattern better than it fits any other HC on this list.**

## SCHEME ENVIRONMENT

### Offensive System

- **Base scheme:** West Coast Offense. Reid learned the WCO under Mike Holmgren in Green Bay, who learned it from Bill Walsh in San Francisco. Reid is installing the Holmgren/Walsh WCO in Philadelphia.
- **Core principles:** Timing-based passing, progression reads, horizontal stretches, ball-control philosophy, short-to-intermediate passing game as the foundation. The WCO uses the pass to set up the run, not the reverse.
- **Personnel groupings:** Multiple personnel packages. WCO uses TEs extensively — 12 personnel, motion, TE trades, TE in the passing game as a high-percentage target. Reid values TE versatility from his years coaching TEs in Green Bay.
- **Terminology:** Walsh/Holmgren WCO language. Specific, detailed, concept-heavy. Play names are long and encode formation, motion, protection, and route concepts. Learning the terminology is a significant investment — this is one of the most complex play-calling languages in football.
- **1999 QB situation:** Reid is expected to draft a quarterback with the #2 overall pick in the April 1999 draft. Donovan McNabb (Syracuse) is the consensus pick. Until the draft, Doug Pederson — a veteran backup who followed Reid from Green Bay — is the starting QB. Pederson knows the WCO; the rookie will learn it.
- **Year 1 offensive expectation:** The offense will be learning a new system from scratch. Execution will be poor early. Reid understands this — he is building for year 2 and beyond, not year 1.

### What Stone Already Understands

- **Passing game concepts (general):** Strong. Stone played in a passing offense and understands route theory, coverage reads, and timing.
- **TE versatility / multiple alignments:** Strong. Directly relevant — Reid's WCO uses TEs in diverse roles.
- **Route craft and stem concepts:** Elite (90 rating). The WCO passing game depends on precise route running — Stone's strongest teaching skill aligns with the system's core demand.
- **Blocking fundamentals:** Competent (75 inline blocking rating). WCO requires TEs to block in the run game and pass-protect.

### What Stone Would Need to Learn

- **WCO terminology:** Completely new. Stone played in a Coryell-influenced system. Walsh/Holmgren WCO language is different — different play-call structure, different route names, different protection calls. This is the steepest learning curve.
- **WCO progression reads and timing concepts:** Stone understands passing concepts but not WCO-specific progressions. The WCO's quarterback decision tree is more structured than the Coryell read system Stone played in.
- **Coaching-specific film and meeting protocols:** Same gap as at every other opening — Stone has never graded film, run a meeting, or organized a practice from the coaching side.
- **Install process:** The WCO install is methodical and sequential. Stone would need to learn how Reid builds the playbook week by week.

### How Steep the Learning Curve Is

**Steeper than Cleveland or San Diego on the scheme side.** The WCO is a more complex, terminology-heavy system than Palmer's hybrid or Riley's college-influenced offense. Stone's Coryell background shares some passing-game DNA with the WCO but the language, structure, and teaching methodology are different.

**However:** Reid is installing the system from scratch. Everyone is learning together. Stone is not behind established WCO coaches — he is learning alongside an entire staff, most of whom are also new to Reid's version of the system. This levels the playing field more than it would at a team with an established WCO staff.

### Why This System Helps Stone's Development

The WCO is the most influential offensive system in modern football. Learning it gives Stone a credential and a schematic vocabulary that transfers to any future coaching job. Reid's version is detailed, structured, and teaching-oriented — the WCO is designed to be taught, which matches Stone's teaching instincts. The system demands precise TE play (route discipline, blocking assignments, alignment accuracy), which is exactly what Stone knows best from the player side.

### Why This System Could Hurt Stone's Development

If Stone cannot learn the WCO terminology fast enough, he becomes a liability instead of an asset. The WCO is unforgiving of imprecision — wrong terminology means wrong execution. Stone's elite football IQ helps, but there is no shortcut to memorizing a playbook. The volume of new information is high, and Reid expects his staff to know the system cold.

## ROLE SLOT: OFFENSIVE QUALITY CONTROL

### Opening Metadata

- **Opening title:** Offensive Quality Control Coach
- **Level:** 0 (GA/QC)
- **Salary range:** $30,000–$45,000
- **Tier:** REALISTIC
- **Competition:** Low-Moderate
- **Hire window:** February–March 1999

### Who Stone Is Replacing

- **Sim logic:** Stone fills the Offensive QC slot. In the sim's divergent timeline, whoever would have held this job takes a different path. Reid's hiring calculus: he gets a Hall of Fame TE with zero coaching experience and elite teaching instincts. Reid's stated priority is teachers and high-character people — Stone fits that description on paper, but Reid will need to be convinced Stone's ego can handle the subordinate role.
- **Reid's QC expectation:** Reid wants QC coaches who are sponges — people who absorb the system, do their work reliably, and don't need to be managed. He does not want QC coaches who need attention, validation, or special treatment. He wants grinders who happen to be smart.

### Daily Duties

- Break down opponent film for the offensive staff (primary duty — WCO film breakdown follows specific protocols for charting routes, protections, and coverage tendencies)
- Organize and run scout-team periods during practice
- Handle administrative tasks: playbook production, meeting material preparation, schedule coordination
- Prepare cut-ups and film edits for position meetings
- Support game-plan preparation with tendency charts and statistical breakdowns
- Attend all offensive meetings — absorb, learn, take notes
- Learn the WCO playbook alongside the players — Stone must know the system to break down film accurately
- Run errands. Handle logistics. Do whatever is asked without complaint.

### Meeting Authority

- None. QC coaches do not lead meetings in Reid's structure. Stone attends as a listener and learner. He may be asked to present film breakdowns to the staff once trust is established. Reid's meeting structure is formal and organized — he learned it from Holmgren, who learned it from Walsh. Meetings start on time, follow an agenda, and are teaching-focused.

### Practice Authority

- Scout team management. Stone runs the scout team to simulate opposing defenses. He does not coach technique during team or individual periods. His practice role is to provide accurate looks, not to teach.

### Film Breakdown Responsibility

- Primary. Core of the QC job. WCO film breakdown is more structured than generic film work — Reid's staff charts specific tendencies, route combinations, and protection patterns. Stone's film literacy (85 rating) is an asset, but he must learn the WCO-specific charting language and methodology.

### Scheme Authority

- Zero. Level 0 coaches have no input on scheme. Stone executes what Reid and Dowhower design.

### Reports To

- Rod Dowhower (OC) for daily offensive assignments. Andy Reid for overall staff accountability. In practice, Stone reports to whoever assigns his film work — which could be Reid directly, Dowhower, or a senior position coach depending on the week's needs.

## ROLE SLOT: TIGHT ENDS COACH (REACH)

### Opening Metadata

- **Opening title:** Tight Ends Coach
- **Level:** 1 (Position Coach)
- **Salary range:** $100,000–$175,000
- **Tier:** REACH
- **Competition:** Moderate
- **Hire window:** February–March 1999

### Why This Is a Reach — But a Different Kind of Reach

Reid hired Pat Shurmur for this job. Shurmur had never coached in the NFL. He spent eight years at Michigan and one year at Stanford — all at the college level, coaching TEs, OL, and special teams. Shurmur had no more NFL coaching experience than Stone has.

This changes the calculus. At Cleveland, Stone would be competing against Ray Perkins — a former NFL head coach. At Philadelphia, the TE coach hire went to a college coach with zero NFL experience. Reid's hiring pattern demonstrates he is willing to hire non-NFL coaches into position-coach roles based on teaching ability and character rather than NFL pedigree.

**Does this make the TE coach job realistic for Stone?** Not quite. Shurmur had something Stone doesn't — structured coaching experience. He had run meetings, designed practice plans, graded film, managed a position group, and taught within a system at the college level for nearly a decade. Stone has done none of those things at any level. The gap between "never coached in the NFL" and "never coached at all" is significant.

**But it is less of a reach here than anywhere else.** Reid's willingness to hire non-NFL coaches means Stone's lack of NFL coaching experience is less disqualifying here than at teams that require it. The remaining question is whether Reid would hire someone with zero coaching experience of any kind — and the answer is probably no for a position coach, but possibly yes for a QC role with an understanding that the TE coach track is available if Stone proves himself.

### Position Room Context (Expected)

- **Chad Lewis** is the starting TE. Lewis was signed as an undrafted free agent by the Rams in 1997, released, and signed by the Eagles. He is a competent but unspectacular player — good hands, limited athleticism, professional attitude. He is a development project who could become a reliable starter with good coaching.
- **TE room depth:** Thin. The Eagles' TE group is not deep or talented. This is a room that needs coaching more than it needs talent management.
- **What the room needs:** Technique refinement, route discipline within the WCO, blocking reliability, and confidence. Lewis and the TEs need a coach who can teach the WCO TE assignments — which are complex — and develop fundamental skills.

## STONE FIT — OFFENSIVE QC

### Why It Fits

- **Philosophical match is the strongest on the board.** Reid's stated hiring priority — teachers and high-character people — aligns directly with Stone's profile. Reid does not care about coaching pedigree; he cares about teaching instincts, coachability, and character.
- **Reid's hiring pattern proves he means it.** He hired a D3 OC, a college coach with no NFL experience as TE coach, a former player with two years of QC experience as his LB coach. Stone's "HOF player, zero coaching experience" profile is unusual, but Reid's 1999 hiring is the most receptive environment for unusual profiles in the entire NFL.
- **Ground-floor opportunity.** Reid is building everything from scratch. Stone enters at the same time as everyone else — no incumbent hierarchy to navigate, no established culture to conform to. Everyone is learning Reid's system together.
- **Reid values the TE position.** He coached TEs in Green Bay (1992–1996). He understands the position's importance in the WCO. Stone's TE expertise has value to Reid in a way it might not to a HC who views the position as secondary.
- **WCO is the most transferable system credential in football.** Learning the Walsh/Holmgren WCO gives Stone a schematic language and teaching framework that is valuable at any future stop. This is the best possible system for long-term coaching development.
- **Low win expectations reduce pressure.** The Eagles are coming off 3-13. Nobody expects them to compete in 1999. Stone can learn without the weight of playoff expectations.
- **Reid's mentorship capacity is high.** Reid learned from Holmgren, who learned from Walsh. The teaching lineage is elite. Reid is explicitly committed to developing his coaches, not just his players. He is — by his own description and by the testimony of every assistant who has worked for him — a teacher of teachers.

### Why It Might Fail

- **WCO learning curve is the steepest scheme challenge on this list.** Stone has zero WCO knowledge. The terminology is dense, the concepts are specific, and the system requires precision. If Stone can't absorb the language fast enough, he can't do the QC job effectively (film breakdown requires system knowledge).
- **Philadelphia media is a hostile environment for a newcomer.** Stone's HOF status makes him visible even in a QC role. If the team struggles — and it will — the media scrutiny is relentless. Stone has never experienced media pressure as a coach, where he is accountable for the product without the ability to perform his way out of criticism.
- **Reid is also learning.** This is Reid's first HC job. He is figuring out how to run an organization, manage a full staff, deal with ownership, handle media, and install a system simultaneously. He may not have bandwidth to personally mentor a QC coach, no matter how talented.
- **No personal connection to Reid.** Stone has no relationship with Reid, no connection to the Holmgren/Walsh tree, no inside track. He is cold-calling. Reid's hiring pattern shows he's open to outsiders, but Stone still needs to impress in an interview — and Reid's interview standard is high (he brought a five-inch binder to his own interview).
- **The ego question is the biggest risk.** Reid explicitly wants low-ego assistants. Stone is a Hall of Fame player. Reid will ask himself: can this man copy playbooks, run scout team, fetch coffee, and take orders from coaches with a fraction of his football credentials — and do it without resentment? If Reid senses any entitlement, the door closes.

### Hidden Upside

- Reid is one of the most highly regarded coaching minds of his generation. His Holmgren/Walsh lineage is elite. Getting into Reid's coaching tree early — before he has proven himself as an HC — is an opportunity whose value is not yet apparent but could be enormous.
- Reid's staff, despite their unremarkable résumés, may develop into exceptional coaches under Reid's mentorship. Stone would be part of that peer cohort.
- The Eagles hold the #2 pick in the 1999 draft. Reid is expected to draft a franchise QB. Stone would witness the development of a drafted quarterback from day one under an HC who is one of football's best QB developers.
- Reid's loyalty to early staff members is a known trait. Coaches who earn his trust and join him early tend to have long tenures.

### Hidden Risk

- If Reid fails as HC (unlikely based on his talent, but unproven as of January 1999), Stone is job-hunting with one year of QC experience on a bad team.
- The WCO is Reid's system — if Stone's future career goes to a non-WCO team, the system-specific learning is less transferable than the coaching fundamentals. But coaching fundamentals learned under Reid transfer everywhere.
- Reid's staff will eventually become internally competitive. Multiple ambitious coaches will seek advancement. Early entry does not guarantee advancement — performance does.

## EVALUATION — OFFENSIVE QC

### How Success Is Measured in Year 1

- **WCO system knowledge:** Does Stone learn the terminology, play calls, and concepts fast enough to break down film accurately? Can he speak the language in meetings without hesitation?
- **Film quality:** Are Stone's breakdowns accurate, thorough, and delivered on time? Does the staff trust his film work?
- **Scout-team precision:** Does Stone's scout team give the starters accurate WCO looks?
- **Reliability:** Does Stone show up early, stay late, do every menial task without complaint?
- **Teaching signals:** Does Stone demonstrate teaching ability in informal moments — correcting a scout-team TE's route, explaining a concept to a young player, adding value in ways that go beyond the QC job description?
- **Ego management:** Does Stone accept the subordinate role without resentment? Does he defer to coaches with less football knowledge but more coaching experience? Does he treat the QC role as a learning opportunity, not a stepping stone to endure?
- **Coachability:** Is Stone himself coachable? Reid values people who absorb feedback, ask questions, and improve. If Stone's QC work gets corrected, does he fix it or defend it?

### What Gets Him Promoted

- Mastering the WCO system faster than expected — demonstrating that his football IQ translates to system acquisition
- Adding visible value in the TE room — if Stone's observations about TE technique or route execution help the TE coach (Shurmur or equivalent), Reid will notice
- Demonstrating teaching ability with players — if Stone can make a scout-team player better, he can make a position-room player better
- Reid or a senior coach vouching for Stone's readiness to take on position-coach responsibilities
- Staff turnover creating openings — if a position coach leaves, Stone's TE expertise makes him a natural internal candidate

### What Gets Him Ignored

- Doing the job competently but invisibly. Reid values initiative and curiosity — "his note taking, his questions," as Holmgren described young Reid. If Stone does the work but shows no intellectual curiosity about the system, he becomes background noise.
- Not building relationships across the staff. Reid's culture is collaborative. If Stone stays siloed, he misses the peer bonds that define this staff's future.
- Failing to learn the WCO fast enough. If Stone's film breakdowns require constant correction because he doesn't know the system, he becomes a burden instead of an asset.

### What Gets Him Fired or Frozen Out

- **Ego.** The single biggest threat to Stone's success on this staff. Reid's culture cannot absorb a QC coach who acts like a star. If Stone carries himself as a Hall of Famer doing Reid a favor by showing up, he is done immediately.
- **Unreliability.** Reid's meetings start on time. Film work is due when it's due. One missed deadline erodes trust. Repeated misses end the relationship.
- **Undercutting the TE coach.** If Stone's TE knowledge manifests as correcting the TE coach in meetings or on the field, the dynamic is poisoned. Stone must channel his expertise through the chain of command.
- **Resistance to the system.** If Stone approaches the WCO with a "my old system was better" attitude — even subtly — Reid will sense it. Reid demands full buy-in to his system. Partial commitment is worse than no commitment.
- **Complaining.** About the losing, the pay, the hours, the facilities, the role. Reid's culture is optimistic and forward-looking. Cynicism is toxic in Reid's environment.

## PRESSURE BANDS — SIM ENGINE REFERENCE

### Year 1 Expectation Band
- **Expected record range:** 3-13 to 6-10. Improvement over 3-13 is expected but not demanded. Reid is installing a new system with a bad roster.
- **Offense expectation:** Bottom-third. New system, new QB (likely a rookie), bad roster. Offensive growing pains are expected.
- **Staff evaluation threshold:** Reid is not expected to win. He is expected to show competence, organization, and a plan. Lurie and Banner will evaluate process, not record. If the team looks coached — prepared, disciplined, improving week-to-week — Reid keeps his job regardless of wins.

### Year 2 Expectation Band
- **Expected record range:** 6-10 to 9-7. Meaningful improvement required. If the QB develops and the system takes hold, this team could surprise. If not, pressure mounts.
- **Staff stability:** Lurie and Banner are patient. Reid's job is likely safe through year 2 unless the team is catastrophically bad and shows no development.

### Year 3+ (if Reid survives)
- **Expected record range:** 8-8 to playoff contention. By year 3, the system should be installed, the QB should be developing, and the roster should be improving through the draft. If the Eagles are still bottom-third, questions will arise.
- **Probability Reid reaches year 3:** High. Lurie and Banner's patience, combined with Reid's organizational ability, suggest he gets at least three years. But this is January 1999 — nothing is guaranteed.

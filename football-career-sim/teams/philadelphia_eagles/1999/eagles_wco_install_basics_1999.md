## WHAT THE 1999 EAGLES OFFENSE IS TRYING TO BE

Reid is installing the Walsh/Holmgren West Coast Offense — the passing game as the foundation of the offense. The WCO uses short-to-intermediate timing routes to move the ball efficiently, creates mismatches through formation and motion, and treats the run game as a complement to the pass rather than the other way around.

The 1999 version will be imperfect. Reid is a first-time play-caller installing a complex system with a bad roster. The offense will struggle. But the schematic foundation is elite — this is the same system that produced multiple Super Bowl championships in San Francisco and Green Bay.

---

## WCO CORE PRINCIPLES

**1. The pass sets up the run.** Traditional offenses establish the run to open the pass. The WCO reverses this. Short, high-percentage passes function like run plays — they move the chains, control the clock, and keep the defense honest. The run game exploits the soft boxes that result from the defense respecting the pass.

**2. Timing and rhythm.** WCO passes are thrown on time to a spot, not held until a receiver gets open. The QB drops back a set number of steps (3-step, 5-step, 7-step), hits a defined rhythm point, and delivers. Routes are designed to break at precise depths that sync with the QB's drop. If the timing breaks, the play breaks.

**3. Progression reads.** The QB reads defenders in a defined sequence — not "look for the open guy" but "read the flat defender; if he drops, throw the flat; if he drives the flat, go to the dig behind him." Every pass concept has a built-in decision tree.

**4. Horizontal stretches.** The WCO attacks the width of the field. Route combinations are designed to put two or three receivers on one defender — forcing the defense to choose who to cover. The QB reads the defender, not the receivers.

**5. Personnel diversity.** Multiple formations and personnel groupings create pre-snap confusion. The same play can be run from 11 personnel (1 RB, 1 TE, 3 WR), 12 personnel (1 RB, 2 TE, 2 WR), 21 personnel (2 RB, 1 TE, 2 WR), or other groupings. Motion and trades (TEs moving from one side to another) create further complexity.

**6. Ball control through the air.** A completed 6-yard pass on 2nd-and-8 is as valuable as a 6-yard run. The WCO treats short passes as extensions of the run game — high-percentage, low-risk, chain-moving plays that consume clock and sustain drives.

## WCO TERMINOLOGY RULES

### Play-Call Structure

WCO play calls are long because they encode multiple variables in a single call. A typical call includes:

**Formation — Personnel — Motion (if any) — Protection — Play/Route Concept**

Example (simplified): `"Spread Right Slot — Zin — 5-Jet — X-Cross"`

- **Spread Right Slot** = formation (spread, strength to the right, slot receiver)
- **Zin** = motion or adjustment tag
- **5-Jet** = protection scheme (5-man protection with jet release by the back)
- **X-Cross** = route concept (the X receiver runs a crossing route)

**What Stone must understand immediately:**
- Every word in the play call has a specific meaning
- Changing one word changes the play
- Film breakdown requires knowing what the call was to evaluate whether it was executed correctly
- Stone cannot grade a rep if he doesn't know the play call

### Position Labels

The WCO uses letter designations for receivers:

- **X** = split end (typically the #1 WR, aligned off the line of scrimmage on the weak side)
- **Z** = flanker (typically the #2 WR, aligned off the line on the strong side)
- **Y** = tight end (on the line of scrimmage, strong side)
- **H** = slot receiver, second TE, or movable piece (varies by formation)
- **F** = fullback
- **T** = tailback / running back

**What Stone already knows:** He played Y his entire career. He understands the TE's alignment and role from a player's perspective. What he doesn't know is how the WCO codes the TE's assignments into the play call.

### Route Numbering

The WCO uses a route tree with numbered routes. The specific numbering can vary by team, but the Holmgren/Walsh standard is:

- **0** = short out / quick out
- **1** = quick hitch / hook
- **2** = slant
- **3** = deep out / comeback
- **4** = deep in / dig
- **5** = option / choice route
- **6** = curl
- **7** = corner / flag
- **8** = post
- **9** = go / fly / streak

**What Stone already knows:** He knows every one of these routes from playing — he ran them for 12 years. What he doesn't know is the WCO numbering convention, how routes are called within the play-call syntax, or how route adjustments are communicated in the WCO language.

### Formation Names

WCO formations have specific names that describe alignment. Examples:

- **Doubles** = two receivers to each side
- **Trips** = three receivers to one side
- **Spread** = wide splits, typically 4-5 WR/TE spread across the field
- **Near / Far** = indicates the position of the TE or back relative to the formation strength
- **Brown / Green / etc.** = specific formation packages (team-specific names)

Reid's specific formation names will be learned during installation. Stone does not need to memorize them before hire — he needs to understand the naming logic so he can learn them efficiently once he's in the building.

## PASSING STRUCTURE — THE THREE LEVELS

### Quick Game (3-step drop)

- Purpose: get the ball out fast, neutralize the pass rush, move the chains
- Routes: hitches, slants, quick outs, quick screens
- TE role: hot route, quick flat, check-down target, or blocking assignment depending on protection
- **What Stone knows from playing:** He ran quick game routes. He understands the timing.
- **What he doesn't know:** How the WCO packages quick game concepts with specific protection calls and sight adjustments against blitz

### Intermediate / Dropback (5-step drop)

- Purpose: attack the middle of the field, create horizontal stretches, exploit zone coverage
- Routes: digs, crossers, curls, option routes, out routes at 12-15 yards
- TE role: primary target in many WCO 5-step concepts. Seam routes, crossing routes, flat/option routes. The TE is often the QB's second or third read in the progression.
- **What Stone knows:** He was a primary intermediate target. He understands the depths, the windows, and the timing.
- **What he doesn't know:** The WCO's specific progression structure — which read is first, which is second, and why

### Deep / Play-Action (7-step drop, play-action)

- Purpose: attack deep, take shots, exploit aggressive run-defense
- Routes: posts, corners, double-moves, deep crossers, seam routes
- TE role: play-action seam route (one of the WCO's most dangerous plays), deep crosser, or max-protect blocker
- **What Stone knows:** He ran deep routes and play-action concepts. The Coryell system emphasizes deep passing.
- **What he doesn't know:** WCO play-action mechanics (the fakes, the boot actions, the naked bootlegs) are different from Coryell play-action

## HOW TEs ARE USED IN REID'S WCO

This section matters most to Stone because it bridges his playing expertise to the system he's learning.

**Y (Inline TE):** The traditional TE aligned on the line of scrimmage. In the WCO, the Y is not just a blocker — he is a receiver who also blocks. Reid's Green Bay experience used the TE extensively:

- **Route running from inline:** Release off the line, run timing routes, convert to receiver within the play-call structure
- **Blocking:** Run-game blocking (base, reach, combo), pass protection (slide protection, chip-and-release, max protect)
- **Motion:** The Y can motion across the formation pre-snap, changing the strength, creating mismatches, or triggering defensive adjustments that the QB reads
- **Trade:** The Y can "trade" — move from one side of the formation to the other between plays, creating schematic flexibility

**H (Move TE / Slot TE):** In 12 personnel, the second TE often aligns in the slot, wing, or flexed position. This TE functions as a receiver first, blocker second. The H creates matchup problems — he's too big for DBs, too fast for LBs.

**What Stone already understands:**
- TE route craft at an elite level (releases, stems, break points, contested catching)
- Inline blocking fundamentals (75 rating — competent, not elite)
- How to read coverage from the TE position
- What makes a TE effective in the passing game (timing, spatial awareness, catch radius)

**What Stone doesn't understand yet:**
- How Reid's WCO codes TE assignments into the play call (the specific language)
- How TE route adjustments work within the WCO's option-route framework (the TE reads the coverage and adjusts his route — the rules are system-specific)
- How the TE's protection responsibilities are communicated in the WCO's protection-call system
- The specific depths, landmarks, and break points Reid teaches (may differ from what Stone learned as a player)

## HOW THE CORYELL-TO-WCO BRIDGE WORKS FOR STONE

Stone played in the Coryell system. The WCO and Coryell systems share DNA but are different philosophies. Understanding the bridge helps Stone learn faster.

**What they share:**
- Multiple receiver sets and formations
- TE as a primary receiving weapon
- Route tree fundamentals (the routes themselves are the same — slant, dig, post, seam)
- Vertical passing concepts (both systems attack deep)
- Timing-based passing

**Where they diverge:**
- **Coryell:** Vertical-first. Stretch the field deep, then work underneath. Routes are often freelance-adjusted based on coverage. Play calls are simpler — less encoded. QB reads are more field-half based (read one side of the field, then come back).
- **WCO:** Horizontal-first. Stretch the field sideline-to-sideline, then attack deep when the defense overcommits to the short game. Routes are more structured — option routes have rules, not instincts. Play calls encode formation, protection, motion, and concept in one long string. QB reads follow defined progressions with a decision tree.

**Stone's translation challenge:**
- He must stop thinking "I know this route" and start thinking "I know this route, but the WCO calls it differently, times it differently, and reads it differently"
- He must learn that the WCO's precision is not rigidity — it is a different kind of freedom. The structure creates efficiency, not limitation.
- He must accept that his Coryell instincts (read the coverage, adjust the route) are the *player's* version of what the WCO does systematically from the *coaching* side

**Dowhower as bridge:** Dowhower started under Coryell and later worked under Walsh. He speaks both languages. If Stone builds a good relationship with Dowhower, the OC can help Stone translate between the two systems faster than anyone else on the staff. This is a hidden developmental asset.
